### Question 1: Discuss and distinguish between proved, dis-proved and not proved.

**Introduction** The concepts of "Proved," "Disproved," and "Not Proved" are the fundamental standards by which a Court evaluates the existence or non-existence of facts. These terms do not imply absolute mathematical certainty but rather refer to the degree of belief or probability that a judge reaches after considering all the materials on record. Under **Section 2** of the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023**, these terms are legally defined to guide judicial reasoning.

**Definition of "Proved"**

According to **Section 2(1)(j)**, a fact is said to be **proved** when, after considering the matters before it, the Court either:

* Believes the fact to exist.  
* Considers its existence so probable that a **prudent man** ought, under the circumstances of the particular case, to act upon the supposition that it exists.

The "prudent man" standard is crucial here. It acknowledges that in human affairs, absolute certainty is rarely possible. Therefore, the law requires "moral certainty." In civil cases, this is generally met by the "preponderance of probability," whereas in criminal cases, a fact is proved only when it is established "beyond reasonable doubt."

**Definition of "Disproved"**

According to **Section 2(1)(c)**, a fact is said to be **disproved** in relation to a fact when, after considering the matters before it, the Court either:

* Believes that the fact does **not** exist.  
* Considers its non-existence so probable that a **prudent man** ought, under the circumstances of the particular case, to act upon the supposition that it does not exist.

"Disproved" is the exact converse of "Proved." It is a positive finding by the Court that a particular fact is false or non-existent.

**Definition of "Not Proved"** According to **Section 2(1)(i)**, a fact is said to be **not proved** when it is neither proved nor disproved.

This is a state of intellectual or judicial "equilibrium" or "flux" where the evidence is either so balanced or so insufficient that the Court cannot reach a firm conclusion of existence or non-existence. It represents a failure on the part of the person who carried the **burden of proof** to convince the Court.

**Distinction Between the Three Terms**

The primary distinction lies in the state of mind of the Court after evaluating the "matters before it":

* **Proved vs. Disproved:** These are mutually exclusive. If a fact is proved, it cannot be disproved in the same proceeding, and vice versa. One establishes the truth of a fact; the other establishes its falsehood.  
* **Proved/Disproved vs. Not Proved:** While "Proved" and "Disproved" result in a definitive finding (Yes or No), "Not Proved" is a neutral state (Uncertain).  
* **Legal Consequence:** If a fact is "Proved," the party asserting it wins that point. If it is "Disproved," the party asserting it loses that point decisively. If it is "Not Proved," the party asserting it still loses that point because they failed to meet the required legal threshold, but the fact is not necessarily declared false.

**Illustrations and Examples**

**Example 1: A Murder Trial**

'A' is accused of murdering 'B'.

* **Proved:** The prosecution produces three eyewitnesses who saw 'A' stab 'B', and the forensic report matches 'A’s' DNA to the blood on the scene. The Court believes 'A' killed 'B'. The fact of murder is **proved**.  
* **Disproved:** 'A' produces records from a hospital in a different city showing he was undergoing surgery at the exact time 'B' was killed. The Court believes 'A' could not have been at the scene. The allegation that 'A' killed 'B' is **disproved**.  
* **Not Proved:** The prosecution brings a witness who "thinks" they saw someone like 'A', but the lighting was poor. 'A' has no alibi but denies the charge. The Court is left in doubt. The murder is **not proved**.

**Example 2: A Suit for Debt**

'X' sues 'Y' for a loan of 10,000 rupees.

* **Proved:** 'X' produces a written and signed promissory note from 'Y' for 10,000 rupees. The debt is **proved**.  
* **Disproved:** 'Y' produces a bank receipt showing he transferred 10,000 rupees back to 'X' with a note "loan repayment." The claim of an outstanding debt is **disproved**.  
* **Not Proved:** 'X' claims it was a cash loan with no witnesses and no receipt. 'Y' simply denies it. There is no evidence either way. The debt is **not proved**.

**Conclusion** These three terms ensure that judicial decisions are not based on whims but on a systematic evaluation of probability. The **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam** maintains this logical framework to ensure a fair trial, placing the responsibility on parties to provide enough evidence to move a fact from the state of "Not Proved" into either "Proved" or "Disproved".

### Question 2: Discuss the theory of relevancy and admissibility. Whether both are same or different?

**Introduction** 

In the law of evidence, the concepts of "Relevancy" and "Admissibility" are the two primary pillars that determine whether a piece of information can be used by a Court to reach a decision. While they are closely linked—relevancy often being a prerequisite for admissibility—they are distinct legal concepts with different foundations. Under the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, these principles ensure that only reliable and legally sanctioned information enters the judicial record.

**The Theory of Relevancy** Relevancy is essentially a matter of logic and human experience. A fact is "relevant" if it has a certain degree of probative value, meaning it helps to prove or disprove the existence of a "fact in issue".

**Meaning**: Relevancy refers to the logical connection between a fact presented in court and the fact in issue. A fact is relevant if it helps the court in determining the truth of the matter in dispute.

* **Illustration**:  
  * If A is tried for murder of B, the fact that A had a motive (like enmity with B) is relevant.  
  * The fact that A was seen near the crime scene at the time of murder is also relevant.  
* **Key Point**: Relevancy is about *logical connection*, not legal permission.

Under **Section 2(1)(k)** of the BSA, a fact is said to be relevant to another when it is connected with the other in any of the ways referred to in the provisions of the Adhiniyam relating to the relevancy of facts. These specific "ways" are detailed in **Chapter II (Sections 4 to 50\)**, which covers various categories such as:

* Facts forming part of the same transaction (Res Gestae).  
* Facts that are the occasion, cause, or effect of facts in issue.  
* Motive, preparation, and previous or subsequent conduct.  
* Opinions of experts.

The BSA follows the principle of **Legal Relevancy**. This means that for a fact to be considered in court, it is not enough for it to be logically connected; it must fall within the specific categories defined by the law.

**Illustration**

* Suppose A is tried for murder of B.  
  * **Logical Relevancy**: The fact that A disliked B is logically relevant.  
  * **Legal Relevancy**: The law recognizes *motive* as a relevant fact, so this is admissible.  
  * But if someone says “A always wore black clothes, and murderers wear black,” that may be logically connected in someone’s mind, but it is not legally relevant under the Act, so the court will reject it.

**The Theory of Admissibility**

Admissibility is a strictly legal concept. It refers to whether a relevant fact is allowed to be received by the Court as evidence. Even if a fact is **logically** or **legally** relevant, the Court may refuse to admit it based on specific rules of exclusion intended to protect public policy, ensure fairness, or maintain the integrity of the judicial process.

**Meaning**: Admissibility refers to whether the court can legally accept a piece of evidence. Even if evidence is relevant, it may not be admissible if barred by law.

**Statutory Basis**: Admissibility is governed by procedural rules and specific provisions (e.g., exclusion of hearsay, privileged communications).

**Principle**: Evidence must not only be relevant but also **comply with legal standards** of admissibility.

**Illustration**:

* A confession made to a police officer is relevant to the crime but is *not admissible* under law (to prevent misuse).  
* A dying declaration, though hearsay, is admissible because the law specifically allows it.

**Section 141** of the **BSA empowers the Judge to decide on admissibility.** When a party proposes to give evidence, the Judge may ask how that fact would be relevant. The Judge shall admit the evidence only if they believe the fact, if proved, would be relevant.

**Difference Between Relevancy and Admissibility**

The distinction between the two can be summarized as follows:

| Feature | Relevancy | Admissibility |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Foundation** | Based on logic and the connection between facts. | Based on strict rules of law and procedure. |
| **Determination** | Determined by the provisions of Sections 4 to 50 of the BSA. | Determined by the Judge under Section 141\. |
| **Scope** | It is the "Genus" (broader category). | It is the "Species" (narrower category). |
| **Sequence** | Relevancy comes first. | Admissibility follows relevancy. |
| **Exclusion** | A fact is irrelevant if it has no logical connection. | A relevant fact can be inadmissible due to specific legal bars. |

**Illustrations and Examples** 

**1\. Confessions to Police (Relevant but Inadmissible)** Suppose 'A' is accused of theft and confesses his crime to a police officer. This confession is logically and legally relevant to the fact in issue (whether 'A' committed the theft). However, **Section 23(1)** of the BSA states that no confession made to a police officer shall be proved against an accused person. Thus, while the statement is **relevant**, it is **inadmissible**.

**2\. Hearsay Evidence (Relevant but Generally Inadmissible)** If 'B' tells the Court, "I heard 'C' say that 'A' committed the murder," this is logically relevant. However, under **Section 55**, oral evidence must generally be **direct**. The witness must be the person who actually saw or heard the fact themselves. Therefore, 'B’s' statement is relevant but is often excluded as inadmissible "hearsay."

**3\. Privileged Communications (Relevant but Inadmissible)** A client tells his advocate that he has committed a crime. This is highly relevant evidence. However, under **Section 132**, an advocate is not permitted to disclose communications made to him for professional service without the client's express consent. The law prioritizes the confidentiality of the lawyer-client relationship over the admission of relevant facts.

**Conclusion**

In summary, Relevancy and Admissibility are not the same. Relevancy is the test of whether a fact is logically connected to the case under the law, whereas Admissibility is the "gatekeeper" that decides if the Court will actually hear it. For a piece of evidence to be successful, it must pass both tests: it must be legally relevant under Sections 4–50 and it must not be barred by any rules of admissibility.

**Relevancy** is about logical connection.

**Admissibility** is about legal permission.

### Question 3: "Facts which, though not in issue, are so connected with a fact in issue, as to form part of the same transaction, are relevant, whether they occurred at the same time and place or at different times and places." Comment and give illustrations.

**Introduction** The statement provided in the question is the verbatim text of **Section 4** of the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023**. This section embodies the legal doctrine of **Res Gestae**, a Latin phrase meaning "things done" or "part of the transaction". It serves as an important exception to the general rule that "hearsay evidence is no evidence," allowing for the admission of statements or acts that are so closely tied to the main event that they are considered a part of it.

**The Concept of "Same Transaction"** The core of this section is the phrase "same transaction". A transaction is not merely a single isolated act but a group of facts so connected together as to be referred to by a single legal name, such as a crime, a contract, or a wrong. For facts to form part of the same transaction, there must be a "continuity of action" and "community of purpose."

Under Section 4, a fact does not need to be a "fact in issue" (the main fact being disputed) to be relevant; it only needs to be so connected to the fact in issue that it forms a part of that same transaction.

**Proximity of Time and Place** A significant feature of Section 4 is that it explicitly states these connected facts are relevant "whether they occurred at the same time and place or at different times and places".

* **Same Time and Place:** These are the most common instances, such as a victim's scream during a physical assault.  
* **Different Times and Places:** If a crime involves a series of linked actions spread across different locations (like a conspiracy or a riot), all those individual acts are relevant as part of the general transaction.

**The Rationale: Spontaneity vs. Concoction** The reason the law admits these facts is their high degree of reliability. When a statement is made or an act is done spontaneously during the heat of the moment, there is little time for the person to "concoct" or fabricate a lie. However, if there is a significant time gap between the event and the statement, it ceases to be part of the *Res Gestae* and becomes a mere "narrated history," which is generally inadmissible.

**Illustrations from the Adhiniyam**

The BSA provides several key illustrations to explain how this connection works in practice:

1. **Murder by Beating:** If 'A' is accused of murdering 'B' by beating him, anything said or done by 'A', 'B', or even bystanders at the time of the beating—or so shortly before or after it as to form part of the transaction—is a relevant fact. For instance, if a bystander shouted, "A is killing B\!" that shout is relevant evidence.  
2. **Waging War:** If 'A' is accused of waging war against the Government of India by participating in an armed insurrection, acts like destroying property, attacking troops, or breaking open jails are all relevant. These are considered part of the "general transaction" even if 'A' was not personally present at every single location where these acts occurred.

   **Example: Waging War: The "Big Picture" Rule**

   Think of this like a **coordinated terrorist attack** or a **large-scale riot**.

   **The Scenario:** Suppose a group plans to overthrow a local government.

   **The Acts:**

1. **Group 1** blows up a bridge at 10:00 AM in the North.  
2. **Group 2** attacks a police station at 10:15 AM in the South.  
3. **Group 3** breaks open a jail in the city center at 11:00 AM.

   **The Legal Logic:** If you are "A" and you were only present at the bridge, the court will still allow evidence of the police station attack and the jailbreak during your trial.

   **Why?** Because all these acts are "connected" as part of one single "transaction"—the war against the state. They show that the bridge explosion wasn't just a random accident, but part of a larger, organized rebellion.

3. **Libel in Correspondence:** In a suit for libel (defamation) contained in a letter, other letters exchanged between the parties that relate to the subject are relevant facts, even if those specific letters do not contain the libelous statement themselves. They help establish the context and "transaction" of the dispute.

   

| Libel in Correspondence: The "Context" Rule Think of this like a hostile email thread or a heated chain of letters. The Scenario: You sue a business rival for a specific letter they sent on Friday that calls you a "thief" (Libel). The Evidence:  Letter 1 (Monday): They complain about a missing shipment. Letter 2 (Wednesday): You reply, defending your work. Letter 3 (Friday): They send the "nasty" letter calling you a thief. The Legal Logic: Even though you are only suing for the "thief" comment in Letter 3, the court will allow Letter 1 and Letter 2 to be used as evidence. Why? Even if Letter 1 and Letter 2 don't contain any insults, they are relevant because they form part of the "correspondence" or "transaction". Without them, the court wouldn't understand *why* the rival sent the third letter or what the dispute was actually about.  |
| :---- |

   

4. **Successive Delivery of Goods:** If there is a question about whether goods were delivered to 'A', and those goods were passed through several intermediate persons, each individual delivery in that chain is a relevant fact.

**Conclusion** Section 4 of the BSA ensures that the Court does not look at a crime or a legal dispute in a vacuum. By allowing the admission of facts that form part of the "same transaction," the law enables a comprehensive understanding of the event. Whether the facts are simultaneous or spread across different locations, their logical and causal connection to the main issue makes them legally relevant for a fair trial.

### Question 4: What is admission? Who can make it? When can it be used by or on behalf of the person making it?

---

**The Definition of Admission**

Under **Section 15** of the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, an admission is defined as a statement which suggests any inference as to any **fact in issue** or **relevant fact**. These statements can be made in three forms: **oral**, **documentary**, or contained in **electronic form**.

In simpler terms, an admission is a "self-harming" statement where a person acknowledges a fact that might go against their own interest in a legal case. The law values admissions because it is highly unlikely that a person would falsely state something that creates a liability for them. It serves as substantive evidence, meaning the court can decide a case based on an admission even without other corroborating evidence.

---

**Who Can Make an Admission?**

The law is very specific about whose statements can actually count as a legal admission. According to **Sections 16, 17, and 18** of the BSA, the **following persons can make admissions**:

1. **Parties to the Proceeding:** This includes the plaintiff and the defendant in a civil case, or the accused in certain contexts.

   These are the main people involved in a lawsuit. 

   **Example:** Imagine a car accident case where a driver is being sued. If that driver tells a witness at the scene, "I'm so sorry, I was looking at my phone and didn't see the red light," that statement is a direct admission by a party to the proceeding.

2. **Agents Authorized by Parties:** A statement made by someone whom the Court regards as expressly or impliedly authorized by the party to speak on their behalf is an admission.

   This is someone you’ve hired or allowed to speak for you. 

   **Example:** If you are selling a house and your real estate agent tells a potential buyer, "Yes, the basement floods every monsoon," that is an admission made by an agent. Because you authorized them to handle the sale, their "bad news" counts as an admission against you in court later.

3. **Parties in a Representative Character:** Statements made by people like trustees or administrators are admissions, but only if they were made while the person actually held that representative character.

   This is for people acting on behalf of someone else, like a trustee or a guardian. 

   **Example:** A person is appointed as the manager of a deceased uncle’s estate. While acting as manager, they tell a bank, "My uncle did owe the 50,000 rupees you’re claiming." This is an admission, but it only counts because they said it while they were officially the manager.

4. **Persons with Proprietary or Pecuniary Interest:** If a person has a financial or property-related interest in the subject matter of the suit, their statement is an admission if it is made while that interest continues.

   This applies to people who have a financial or property-related stake in the case. 

   **Example:** You and your business partner jointly own a restaurant. Your partner tells a food inspector, "We’ve been having issues with the refrigerator temperature lately." Even though you weren't there, this is an admission against you because you both share a financial interest in the business.

   

5. **Predecessors-in-Title:** These are persons from whom the current parties to the suit have derived their interest in the property or subject matter.

   This refers to the person who owned the property before you. 

   **Example:** You buy a plot of land. Ten years ago, the person who sold it to you admitted to a neighbor in a letter, "The back fence is actually 2 feet inside your property." That old statement is an admission that can be used against you today to prove the boundary is wrong.

6. **Persons whose position must be proved (Section 17):** If a person’s liability is central to the case against a party, their statement can be an admission. For example, if you sue a landlord for a manager's mistake, the manager's statement that they made the mistake is an admission against the landlord.

   This is a bit more technical: it's when someone else's mistake is the reason you are being sued. 

   **Example:** You hire a contractor to collect rent from your tenants. You sue the contractor for not collecting rent from "Tenant X." If "Tenant X" admits, "Yes, I did owe rent to the contractor," that statement is an admission that helps prove the contractor failed in their duty.

7. **Referees (Section 18):** If a party to a suit says, "Go and ask 'C'; 'C' knows all about it," then whatever 'C' says becomes an admission by that party.

   This is when you point to a third party as the "source of truth." 

   **Example:** You are selling a used motorcycle. The buyer asks if the engine is in good shape. You say, "Go ask the mechanic down the street; he has serviced this bike for five years and knows everything." If the mechanic tells the buyer the engine is failing, that is an admission by a referee, and it’s legally as if you said it yourself.

---

**When Can an Admission be Used by or on Behalf of the Person Making It?**

The general rule under **Section 19** is that admissions can be proved **against** the person who makes them, but **not by** the person who makes them. This prevents people from manufacturing evidence in their own favor (self-serving statements).

**😀In Simple words:** 

An **admission** is a statement where someone acknowledges a fact against their own interest.

**Section 19 BSA** says:

* Admissions can be used **against** the person who made them.  
* But they cannot be used **by** that person in their own favor.

Purpose: To prevent people from creating “self‑serving” evidence that helps them escape liability.

**👍Example:** Suppose A is accused of stealing B’s phone.

**Admission against interest**:

A says to his friend, “Yes, I took B’s phone.”

* → This statement can be proved in court **against A**, because it shows guilt.

**Self‑serving statement**:

A says, “I never stole B’s phone, I was at home.”

* → This cannot be proved **by A himself** in court, because otherwise anyone could manufacture favorable evidence by simply declaring their innocence outside court.

However, there are **three critical exceptions** where you can use your own admission to help your case:

**1\. Statements equivalent to Dying Declarations** An admission may be used by the person making it if the statement is of such a nature that, if that person were dead, it would be relevant between third persons as a dying declaration or a statement made in the ordinary course of business under **Section 26**.

* **Example:** A ship captain records in his logbook every day that the ship is on the correct course. If he is later sued for negligence, he can use his own logbook entries to prove he was on course, because if he had died, those entries would have been admissible under Section 26\.

**2\. Statements regarding State of Mind or Body** A person can prove their own statement if it describes a then-existing state of mind (like intention or knowledge) or a state of body (like pain or health), provided it was made at the time the feeling existed and is accompanied by conduct that makes a lie unlikely.

* **Example:** 'A' is accused of receiving stolen goods. He can prove that he asked an expert to examine the goods because he doubted their genuineness. This self-serving statement is allowed because it explains his state of mind (good faith) at that time.

**3\. Statements Relevant Otherwise than as an Admission** If a statement is relevant under some other section of the BSA (like Section 4 regarding *Res Gestae*), it can be used even if it happens to be an admission made by the party themselves.

👍**Example:** Suppose A is accused of murdering B.

* Right after the stabbing, B shouts: *“A stabbed me\!”*  
  * This is an **admission by B** (naming A as the killer).  
  * But more importantly, it is part of the **same transaction (Res Gestae, Section 4\)**.  
  * Therefore, it is admissible in court, even though it is technically an admission.

👍Example:

* A writes in his diary: *“I was planning to kill B.”*  
  * This is an admission.  
  * But it is also relevant as evidence of **motive and preparation** under Sections 8–9.  
  * So it can be admitted in court, not just because it’s an admission, but because it falls under another legally recognized category.

---

**Illustrations to Simplify**

**The "Self-Harming" Rule:** If 'A' says to 'B', "I owe you 5,000 rupees," 'B' can use this statement in court to win a case against 'A'. However, 'A' cannot go to court and say, "I wrote in my diary that I already paid 'B'," to prove he is innocent. The diary entry is a self-serving statement and is generally excluded.

**The "Referee" Example:** Suppose you buy a used car from 'X'. 'X' tells you, "The engine is perfect; go ask my mechanic 'M'." When you ask 'M', he says, "The engine is actually failing." In court, 'M's' statement is considered an admission made by 'X' because 'X' expressly referred you to him.

### Question 5: Who is an expert? What is the difference between an expert and ordinary witness?

**Introduction**

In the journey of a trial, a judge is often faced with technical or scientific questions that fall outside the realm of common knowledge—such as determining the cause of a rare disease, identifying a forged digital signature, or analyzing ballistic patterns. In these moments, the law relies on "Experts." Under the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, the testimony of an expert is a specific exception to the general rule that a witness must only testify to facts they have personally perceived, not their opinions.

**Who is an Expert?**

According to **Section 39(1)** of the BSA, when a Court needs to form an opinion on specific points, the opinions of persons "specially skilled" in those areas are considered relevant facts. These areas include:

* **Foreign Law:** Laws of countries other than India.  
* **Science or Art:** This covers a vast range, from medical science to ballistics or even the "art" of forgery.  
* **Identity of Handwriting or Finger Impressions:** Specialists who can determine if a signature or print belongs to a specific person.  
* **Other Specialized Fields:** The BSA adds a broad "any other field" to accommodate evolving expertise.

Furthermore, **Section 39(2)** specifically recognizes an **Examiner of Electronic Evidence** (as referred to in the IT Act, 2000\) as an expert regarding information stored or transmitted in computer resources or digital forms.

**Practical Example of an Expert:** Imagine a case where a person is found dead with a mysterious white powder near their mouth. A **Toxicologist** (the expert) is called. They didn't see the person die, but they analyze the powder and the victim's blood in a lab. They testify, "Based on my 20 years of study, this powder is a specific rare poison that causes heart failure within minutes." The Court treats this opinion as a relevant fact.

**Difference Between an Expert and an Ordinary Witness**

While both appear in court to help find the truth, their roles are legally distinct.

| Feature | Ordinary Witness (Witness of Fact) | Expert Witness (Witness of Opinion) |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Foundation of Knowledge** | Based on personal perception—what they saw, heard, or felt. | Based on specialized skill, study, or experience in a specific field. |
| **Role of Opinion** | Generally prohibited from giving opinions; they must state only the facts. | Their primary role is to provide a professional opinion to assist the Court. |
| **Personal Knowledge of the Event** | Must have been "at the scene" or personally involved in the facts of the case. | Does not need to have been at the scene; they often analyze data or objects later. |
| **Nature of Evidence** | Provides "Direct Evidence" of the facts in issue. | Provides "Advisory Evidence" to help the judge interpret technical facts. |
| **Scientific Testing** | Usually testifies about natural human observations. | May give an account of experiments or tests performed to reach a conclusion. |

**Comparison Example: The Car Crash**

* **The Ordinary Witness:** A tea-stall owner who was standing at the corner. He testifies, "I saw the red car skip the signal and hit the truck at high speed." He is testifying to a fact he perceived with his eyes.  
* **The Expert Witness:** A **Mechanical Engineer** or **Forensic Accident Investigator**. They arrive days later, look at the skid marks on the road and the crush depth of the car's metal. They testify, "Based on the 10-meter skid marks and the metal deformation, the car was traveling at exactly 85 km/h at the moment of impact." They are providing an opinion based on scientific principles.

**Legal Status and Binding Nature**

It is important to remember that an expert's opinion is **not binding** on the judge. It is "consultative" in nature. The Court evaluates the grounds of the opinion under **Section 45**. If the expert's opinion is inconsistent with other proved facts, the Court can reject it. For example, if an expert says a document is 50 years old, but a witness proves they saw the accused writing it yesterday, the expert's opinion may be discarded.

**Conclusion**

In the BSA, the expert acts as a bridge between complex science and the hall of justice. While the ordinary witness provides the "bricks" of the case (the raw facts), the expert provides the "blueprints" (the interpretation of those facts). Both are essential, but the expert is unique because the law trusts their "trained mind" to offer an opinion where an untrained mind would only see confusion.

### Question 6: What is dying declaration? State the grounds on which a dying declaration is admitted in evidence.

---

**Definition of Dying Declaration**

Under the Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023, a dying declaration is governed by **Section 26(a)**. **It is a statement**, either written or verbal, made by a person who is dead, regarding the cause of their death or the circumstances of the transaction that resulted in their death.

A unique feature of Indian law (retained in the BSA) is that such a statement is relevant whether the person was or was not under expectation of death at the time they made it. It is also relevant regardless of the nature of the proceeding (civil or criminal) in which the cause of death is being questioned.

**The Two Key Parts of Section 26(a)**

The law divides these statements into two specific categories:

1. **Cause of Death:** Direct statements about what or who killed the person.  
2. **Circumstances of the Transaction:** Statements about events that eventually led to the death, even if they happened some time before the actual incident.

---

👍**Practical Life Examples**

Example 1: The Direct "Cause of Death"

Imagine a person is rushed to the hospital with severe burn injuries. Before losing consciousness, they tell the doctor, *"My neighbor, Raj, poured kerosene on me and set me on fire because of a land dispute."* **Why it's a Dying Declaration:** This is a direct statement about the **cause** of the injuries that resulted in death.

Example 2: The "Circumstances of the Transaction"

A man tells his wife on Monday, *"I received a letter from Mr. Khanna asking me to come to his farmhouse today to collect the 5 lakh rupees he owes me. I am going there now."* The man never returns, and his body is found in a trunk two days later.

* **Why it's a Dying Declaration:** Even though he wasn't "dying" when he spoke to his wife, his statement explains the **circumstance** (going to meet the debtor) that directly led to the transaction resulting in his death.

Example 3: Non-Verbal Gestures

A woman’s throat has been cut, and she is unable to speak. A police officer asks, *"Did Suresh do this to you?"* She nods her head "Yes" and points toward a photo of Suresh.

* **Why it's a Dying Declaration:** Under the BSA, a declaration can be made through **gestures or signs** if the person is physically unable to speak.

---

**Grounds for Admission (Rationale)**

The admission of a dying declaration is an exception to the general rule against "Hearsay Evidence." The law admits it based on two primary legal maxims and principles:

1. **Nemo moriturus praesumitur mentiri:** This Latin maxim means "**a man will not meet his Maker with a lie in his mouth**". The law assumes that a person who is on the verge of death has no motive to tell a lie, as the solemnity of the occasion provides a guarantee of truth equivalent to an oath.  
2. **Principle of Necessity:** Often, the victim is the only eyewitness to the crime. If their statement were excluded simply because they are not alive to testify in court, it would lead to a miscarriage of justice. The deceased’s words are admitted because they are the "best evidence" available under the circumstances.

---

**Essential Conditions for Admissibility**

For a statement to be considered a valid dying declaration under **Section 26(a)**, the following conditions must be met:

* **Death of the Declarant:** The person who made the statement must be dead. If the person survives, the statement cannot be used as a dying declaration (though it might be used to corroborate or contradict their testimony under other sections).  
* **Cause of Death or Circumstances of Transaction:** The statement must relate to the cause of death or the circumstances leading to it. For example, a statement about a family dispute six months prior might not be relevant unless it was directly connected to the final fatal event.  
* **Cause of Death must be in Issue:** The declaration is only relevant in cases where the court is actually inquiring into why that person died.  
* **Competency of Declarant:** The person making the statement must have been in a "fit state of mind" to understand what they were saying. Usually, a medical certificate of fitness is attached to such declarations in practice.

---

**Illustrations and Practical Examples**

**Example 1: The Assault (Illustration (a) from BSA)** The question is whether 'A' was murdered by 'B'. 'A' dies of injuries. Shortly before dying, 'A' tells a neighbor, "B stabbed me because I refused to give him money." This statement is a relevant fact as a dying declaration.

**Example 2: The Rape Case** 'A' dies due to injuries received during a transaction in which she was raped. The question is whether she was raped by 'B'. A statement made by 'A' before her death regarding the assault and the identity of the attacker is relevant under this section.

**Example 3: The Burn Case (Practical Example)**

A woman is brought to the hospital with 90% burns. She tells the doctor and the police officer, "My husband and mother-in-law poured kerosene on me and set me on fire." Even if no other eyewitness exists, this statement is admissible as a dying declaration.

---

**Difference Between Indian Law and English Law**

It is important to note that under English Law, a dying declaration is only admissible if the person was in **actual expectation of death**. However, under **Section 26(a)** of the BSA, the statement is relevant even if the person had hope of recovery at the time they spoke.

---

**Evidentiary Value**

While a dying declaration is substantive evidence, courts approach it with caution.

* If it is recorded by a **Magistrate**, it carries higher value.  
* If it is voluntary and not the result of "tutoring" (being told what to say by relatives), a conviction can be based solely on a dying declaration without further corroboration.  
* However, if there are multiple, contradictory dying declarations, the court will look for other evidence to confirm which one is true.

### Question 7: Define judicial notice and discuss the circumstance in which court shall take the judicial notice.

---

**Definition and Concept of Judicial Notice**

Generally, the rule of evidence is that "he who asserts must prove." Every fact used to support a case must be proved through witnesses or documents. However, **Judicial Notice** is the major exception to this rule. It is a rule of judicial convenience and common sense based on the idea that certain facts are so notorious, clearly established, or officially documented that requiring formal proof would be a waste of the Court's time.

Under **Section 51** of the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, it is clearly stated that no fact of which the Court will take judicial notice needs to be proved. By taking judicial notice, the Judge simply accepts the fact as true without requiring any evidence from the parties.

---

**Circumstances Where the Court "Shall" Take Judicial Notice**

While some facts are at the discretion of the Judge, **Section 52(1)** of the BSA lists specific circumstances where the Court is **mandated** (it *shall*) to take judicial notice. These include:

1. **All Laws in Force:** This includes all Indian laws, including those with extra-territorial operation.

   **Practical Example:** In your consumer case against LG, your lawyer doesn’t need to bring a witness to "prove" that the **Consumer Protection Act** exists. The Judge is legally required to know the laws of the land just as you are expected to know the speed limit while driving.

2. **International Agreements:** Any international treaty, agreement, or convention involving India, as well as decisions made by India in international bodies.

   **Practical Example:** If a case involves a cross-border dispute about carbon emissions, the Court already knows about the **Paris Agreement**. You don’t need to fly in a UN official to testify that India signed it.

3. **Legislative Proceedings:** The course of proceedings of the Constituent Assembly of India, Parliament, and State Legislatures.

   **Practical Example:** If there is a dispute about what was discussed during the passing of a new Bill in the **Lok Sabha**, the Court can simply refer to the official "Hansard" (transcripts) of Parliament. It treats these records as fact without requiring a member of Parliament to come and testify.

4. **Official Seals:** The seals of all Courts and Tribunals in India, as well as seals of Courts of Admiralty, Notaries Public, and any seals authorized by the Constitution or Parliament.

   **Practical Example:** If you present a document stamped with the seal of the **High Court of Allahabad**, the Judge accepts it as genuine immediately. They won't ask, "How do I know this is the real seal?"—they are mandated to recognize it.

5. **Public Officers:** The names, titles, functions, and signatures of persons currently filling any public office in a State, provided their appointment was notified in an Official Gazette.

   **Practical Example:** If a document is signed by the **District Magistrate (DM)** of Raebareli, and that DM's appointment was notified in the **Official Gazette**, the Court takes notice of their name and signature. You don't have to prove that the DM is actually the DM.

   

6. **Foreign Countries:** The existence, title, and national flag of every country or sovereign recognized by the Government of India.

   **Practical Example:** If a contract mentions "The Republic of France," you don’t need to bring a geography professor to prove that France is a real country or that its flag is blue, white, and red. The Court accepts the existence of all countries recognized by India.

7. **Time and Geography:** The divisions of time (calendar), geographical divisions of the world, and public festivals, fasts, or holidays notified in the Official Gazette.

   **Practical Example:** If a crime happened on **Holi**, you don’t need to prove that Holi was a public holiday that year. Similarly, the Court knows that the sun rises in the east and that **Raebareli** is south of Lucknow without needing a map as "evidence."

8. **Territory of India:** The physical boundaries and extent of the territory of India.

   **Practical Example:** If a dispute arises about whether a specific village in **Arunachal Pradesh** is in India, the Court takes judicial notice of the national boundaries. It won't ask for a witness to prove that the land belongs to the Indian territory.

9. **Hostilities:** The commencement, continuation, and termination of hostilities (war) between India and any other country or body of persons.

   **Practical Example:** If a business fails to deliver goods because a war broke out between India and another nation, the business owner doesn't have to "prove" the war is happening. The Court takes notice of the state of war or peace as a public fact.

10. **Officers of the Court:** The names of the members and officers of the Court, their deputies, assistants, and advocates authorized by law to appear before it.

    **Practical Example:** The Judge knows the names of the **Advocates** standing before them and the **Court Clerk** sitting in front of them. A lawyer doesn't have to prove they are an officer of that specific Court.

11. **Rule of the Road:** Whether on land or at sea, the basic rules of traffic and navigation.

    **Practical Example:** In a motor accident claim, no one has to prove that "in India, we drive on the left side of the road". The Court accepts this basic rule of traffic as a fact that requires no further evidence.

---

**Practical Life Examples**

Consider these scenarios where a lawyer doesn't need to bring a witness:

**1\. The "Rule of the Road" Example** If a case involves a car accident on a one-way street, the lawyer does not need to bring a traffic expert to prove that "in India, vehicles drive on the left side of the road." The Court already knows this and **shall** take judicial notice of it under Section 52(1)(l).

**2\. The "Public Holiday" Example** Suppose a contract was supposed to be signed on August 15th. One party argues they couldn't sign it because it was a national holiday. They don't need to prove that August 15th is Independence Day; the Court takes judicial notice of public holidays notified in the Gazette.

**3\. The "Law of the Land" Example** A lawyer in your Consumer Protection case against LG does not need to "prove" that the Consumer Protection Act exists. The Judge is legally bound to know the laws of India.

---

**The Requirement of "Aid" (Section 52(2))**

Even though the law says the Court "shall" take notice, a Judge is still a human being and may not remember every detail (like the specific seal of a remote foreign court).

* **Resort to Books:** The Court may refer to appropriate books or documents for help in these matters, as well as matters of public history, science, or art.  
* **Refusal to Act:** If a party asks the Court to take judicial notice of a fact, the Judge can refuse to do so until that person produces the book or document the Judge needs to be sure.

**Conclusion** Judicial notice is a powerful tool that streamlines the trial process. For your 4th-semester exams, remember that **Section 51** is the "Permission" (no proof needed) and **Section 52** is the "Checklist" of facts the Court is forced to accept.

### Question 8: Define Estoppel. What are the essential ingredients of estoppel?

---

**The Concept of Estoppel** Estoppel is a rule of equity and evidence that prevents a person from "blowing hot and cold" at the same time. In legal terms, it means that if you make a statement or act in a way that leads someone else to believe something and they act on that belief, you cannot later change your story to their disadvantage. It is not a cause of action (you can't usually sue someone *just* for estoppel), but it is a powerful "shield" used in litigation to stop a party from denying a previous stand. Under the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, Estoppel is covered in **Chapter VIII**, spanning **Sections 121 to 123**.

👏the concept: 

Estoppel is a legal principle designed to ensure fairness by preventing a person from going back on their word, representation, or conduct when another party has relied on it to their detriment. In short, it stops parties from contradicting themselves in legal proceedings.

**👍Example**: 

Suppose you are standing on a plot of land that actually belongs to your father. A buyer walks up and asks if you own it. You say, **"Yes, this is my land,"** even though you know it isn't yours yet. The buyer believes you and pays you the full price.

A month later, your father passes away and leaves that exact plot of land to you in his will. Now that you are the legal owner, you realize the land is worth much more than what the buyer paid. You go to court and argue, **"The sale I made last month is void because I didn't actually own the land when I signed the papers."**

The Court will apply **Estoppel** and tell you: **"No. You intentionally led the buyer to believe you were the owner, and they spent their money based on your word. You are now legally stopped from denying that you were the owner."** Even though you were technically lying at the time of the sale, the law forces you to stand by your original statement because the buyer acted on it.

---

**Legal Definition under Section 121** According to **Section 121** of the BSA, when one person has, by his **declaration, act, or omission**, intentionally caused or permitted another person to believe a thing to be true and to act upon such belief, neither he nor his representative shall be allowed, in any suit or proceeding between himself and such person or his representative, to deny the truth of that thing. Essentially, the law shuts the mouth of a person who has misled another through their conduct or words.

---

**Essential Ingredients of Estoppel** For the plea of estoppel to be successful in a court of law, four specific ingredients must be satisfied based on **Section 121**:

**1\. There must be a Representation:** The first step is that one person must make a representation to another. This representation can be in the form of a **declaration** (speaking/writing), an **act** (doing something), or an **omission** (staying silent when there was a duty to speak).

* **Practical Example:** You are selling a house. A buyer asks, "Does this property have any legal disputes?" You stay silent even though you know there is a pending court case. Your **omission** (silence) is a representation that the property is clear.

**2\. Intention to Deceive or Permit Belief:** The person making the representation must have **intentionally** caused or permitted the other person to believe the representation to be true. It doesn't always require "fraudulent" intent; even if you intentionally made the statement knowing the other person would rely on it, this ingredient is met.

* **Practical Example:** You tell a friend, "This laptop is brand new," intending for them to believe it so they will buy it from you. You have intentionally created that belief.

**3\. The Other Person Must Act Upon the Belief:** Estoppel only applies if the person receiving the information actually **acts upon that belief**. If they heard your lie but didn't change their behavior because of it, there is no estoppel.

* **Practical Example:** Based on your statement that the laptop is new, your friend pays you 50,000 rupees. Their "act" is the payment of money based on the belief you created.

**4\. Change of Position or Detriment:**

The person acting on the belief must have changed their position such that if the representation were allowed to be denied now, they would suffer a loss.

* **Practical Example:** If you later try to say, "Actually, the laptop was three years old and I want it back," the friend is protected. You are **estopped** from denying your original statement because the friend already paid and would lose their money.

---

**Illustrations and Specific Types of Estoppel**

**The Statutory Illustration (Section 121):** 'A' intentionally and falsely leads 'B' to believe that certain land belongs to 'A', and thereby induces 'B' to buy and pay for it. Later, the land actually becomes the property of 'A', and 'A' tries to cancel the sale by saying he didn't have the title when he sold it. The law says 'A' **must not be allowed** to prove his want of title. He is stuck with his original lie because 'B' acted on it.

**Estoppel of Tenant (Section 122):** No tenant of immovable property is permitted to deny that their landlord had a valid title to the property at the beginning of the tenancy.

* **Practical Example:** If you rent an apartment from Mr. Sharma, you cannot stop paying rent by claiming in court that Mr. Sharma isn't actually the real owner. As long as you are his tenant, you are estopped from questioning his title.

**Estoppel of Licensee (Section 122):** A person who comes onto land by the license (permission) of the person in possession cannot later deny that such person had a title to that possession at the time the license was given.

* **Practical Example:** If your neighbor lets you park your car in his driveway out of kindness, you cannot later claim in a dispute that the neighbor doesn't actually own the driveway.

**Estoppel of Acceptor/Bailee (Section 123):** The acceptor of a bill of exchange cannot deny that the drawer had the authority to draw or endorse it. Similarly, a **bailee** (someone holding goods for another) cannot deny that the **bailor** had the authority to give them those goods at the time the bailment started.

* **Practical Example:** If you leave your car at a workshop for repairs, the mechanic (bailee) cannot later refuse to give it back by claiming you aren't the real owner. He is estopped from denying your authority to leave the car with him.

---

**Conclusion**

Estoppel is essentially a rule of "fair play" in the legal system. It prevents the inconsistency of parties and ensures that if someone relies on your word to their detriment, you are held to that word. For your exams, remember that **Section 121** is the general rule, **Section 122** is for Landlords/Tenants, and **Section 123** is for Bailees and Bill of Exchange transactions.

### Question 9: What are leading questions? When can leading questions be asked?

The definition of a **leading question** is found in **Section 146(1)** of the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023**. 

A **leading question** is any question that suggests the specific answer which the person asking it wishes or expects to receive. In simpler terms, it is a question that "puts words into the witness's mouth" or can be answered with a simple "Yes" or "No."

**Example of a Leading Question:** *"Was the defendant wearing a red jacket when he pulled out the knife?"* This is leading because it suggests the jacket was red and that a knife was pulled. 

**Example of a Non-Leading Question:** *"What was the defendant wearing, and what did he do next?"* This allows the witness to describe the events in their own words.

The rules for when these questions can be asked are divided into three parts:

**1\. When Leading Questions Must Not Be Asked (Section 146(2)):** Leading questions **must not**, if objected to by the opposite party, be asked in an **examination-in-chief** or a **re-examination**, except with the **permission of the Court**. This is because the party calling the witness wants the witness to tell their own story, not just agree with the lawyer's suggestions.

**2\. When the Court Shall Permit Them (Section 146(3)):**

The Court **shall permit** leading questions for matters that are:

* **Introductory:** Basic details like name, age, or address.  
* **Undisputed:** Facts that both sides already agree are true.  
* **Sufficiently Proved:** Matters that have already been clearly established by other evidence.

**3\. When Leading Questions May Be Asked (Section 146(4)):** Leading questions **may be asked** freely during **cross-examination**. This is because the purpose of cross-examination is to test the witness's truthfulness and "shake" their story, so the lawyer is allowed to be more suggestive and aggressive.

In conclusion, the legal framework for **leading questions** under **Section 146** of the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023**, is designed to maintain the integrity of oral evidence while allowing for a rigorous search for the truth. By generally prohibiting these questions during **examination-in-chief** and **re-examination**, the law ensures that a witness provides their own spontaneous account of the facts rather than merely nodding along to the lawyer's suggestions. However, the broad permission to use leading questions during **cross-examination** is essential for "shaking the credit" of a witness and exposing potential falsehoods. The **Court's power** to permit such questions in introductory or undisputed matters further ensures that judicial time is not wasted on trivialities. For your exams, remember that the ultimate goal of these rules is to ensure that the "voice" heard in the courtroom is that of the witness, not the advocate.

### Question 10: When can improper admission or rejection of evidence be ground for retrial or reversal of the decision in any case?

**Introduction to Section 169** Under the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, the rules regarding the consequences of mistakes made by a trial court in admitting or rejecting evidence are governed by **Section 169**. This section is based on the principle of "substantial justice," meaning that a higher court will not overturn a judgment or order a retrial just because of a technical error in evidence, provided the final outcome of the case was correct.

**The General Rule: No New Trial Based on Evidence Errors Alone**

**Section 169** explicitly states that the **improper admission** or **improper rejection** of evidence shall **not** be a ground, by itself, for a new trial or a reversal of a decision. The law prioritizes the finality of litigation over minor procedural or evidentiary mistakes. A retrial or reversal is only granted if the error actually resulted in a miscarriage of justice.

**Condition 1: Improper Admission of Evidence** If a piece of evidence was wrongly admitted (e.g., hearsay or a statement that should have been excluded), the decision will **not** be reversed if there was **sufficient other evidence**, independent of the wrongly admitted part, to justify the Court's decision.

* **Practical Example:** Suppose in a murder trial, the Court wrongly admits a confession made to a police officer. However, the prosecution also produced three independent eyewitnesses and a forensic report matching the weapon to the accused. Even if the confession is removed from the record, the remaining evidence is enough to prove the guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. In this case, there will be no retrial because the decision is still justified.

**Condition 2: Improper Rejection of Evidence** If the Court wrongly refused to accept a valid piece of evidence, the decision will **not** be reversed if the Court finds that even if that rejected evidence had been received, it **ought not to have varied (changed)** the decision.

* **Practical Example:** Imagine a civil suit for a debt of 10 lakh rupees. The defendant tries to produce a receipt for a small payment of 1,000 rupees, but the Judge wrongly rejects it. However, the defendant has already admitted to the rest of the debt and has no other defense. Since accepting that 1,000-rupee receipt would not change the fact that the defendant owes the remaining 9,99,000 rupees, the rejection is considered a "harmless error," and the decision stands.

**The Test for Higher Courts** When an appeal is filed based on evidentiary errors, the Appellate Court performs a "balancing test". It asks: "If we remove the wrongly admitted evidence, is there still enough proof?" or "If we add the wrongly rejected evidence, does the winner of the case change?" If the result remains the same in both scenarios, the higher court will refuse to interfere with the original judgment.

**Conclusion** **Section 169** of the BSA functions as a "safety valve" for the judiciary. It prevents the unnecessary burden of retrials and ensures that the legal system focuses on the **substance of justice** rather than the **perfection of procedure**. For your semester exams, emphasize that the primary goal is to ensure the verdict is correct based on the "total weight" of relevant and proved facts, rather than being distracted by isolated technical mistakes.

### Question 1\. Explain 'Fact in Issue' and 'Relevant Facts' under Law of Evidence. Point out some differences between them.

In the Law of Evidence, the entire judicial process revolves around two central concepts: what needs to be decided (**Fact in Issue**) and what information helps the court reach that decision (**Relevant Facts**). Under the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, these concepts are the filters that determine what the judge will hear and what they will ignore. **Section 3** of the Adhiniyam explicitly states that evidence may be given only of the existence or non-existence of every fact in issue and of relevant facts, and of no others.

**What is a Fact in Issue?**

According to **Section 2(1)(g)** of the BSA, a **Fact in Issue** means and includes any fact from which, either by itself or in connection with other facts, the existence, non-existence, nature, or extent of any right, liability, or disability, asserted or denied in any suit or proceeding, necessarily follows.

In simpler terms, these are the **"main facts"** or **"matters of dispute.**" When one party claims a right (like the right to get a refund for a faulty AC) and the other party denies it, the facts that **settle that disagreement** are the facts in issue. In civil law, these are the "Issues" framed by the judge after looking at the pleadings. In criminal law, they are the ingredients of the charge against the accused.

**Example of Fact in Issue:** If 'A' is accused of the murder of 'B', the facts in issue at the trial would be:

* That 'A' caused 'B’s' death.  
* That 'A' intended to cause 'B’s' death.  
* That 'A' had received grave and sudden provocation from 'B' (if 'A' claims self-defense).

**What is a Relevant Fact?**

A **Relevant Fact** is defined under **Section 2(1)(k)**. A fact is said to be relevant to another when it is connected with the other in any of the ways referred to in the provisions of the Adhiniyam relating to the **relevancy of facts**. These specific connections are detailed in **Sections 4 to 50** of the BSA.

**While a fact in issue is what you are trying to prove, a relevant fact is the "evidence" or "clue" you use to prove it. These are also known as "evidentiary facts." They are not the final point of the case, but they provide the logical link that helps the judge understand the truth.**

**Example of Relevant Fact:** In the same murder case mentioned above, the following would be relevant facts:

* The fact that 'A' bought a knife a day before the murder (**Preparation**).  
* The fact that 'A' was seen running away from 'B’s' house immediately after the crime (**Conduct**).  
* The fact that 'A' and 'B' had a bitter argument an hour before the death (**Motive/Res Gestae**).

**Differences Between Fact in Issue and Relevant Facts**

Here are the primary points of distinction:

**1\. Nature and Role:** A **Fact in Issue** is the "Factum Probandum," which means the "fact to be proved." It is the ultimate goal of the trial. A **Relevant Fact** is the "Factum Probans," which means the "proving fact." It is the tool or the step used to reach the goal.

**2\. Legal Basis:** **Facts in Issue** are determined by the **substantive law** (like the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita for crimes or the Contract Act for civil suits) and the specific **pleadings** of the parties. **Relevant Facts** are determined by the **Law of Evidence** (BSA), specifically Sections 4 to 50\.

**3\. Scope and Relationship:** Every **Fact in Issue** is relevant to the case by its very nature. However, not every **Relevant Fact** is a fact in issue. A relevant fact might be secondary information that only helps build the story.

**4\. Court's Decision:** The final judgment of the court is always a direct finding on the **Facts in Issue**. For example, "Guilty of Murder" or "Not Guilty." The findings on **Relevant Facts** are merely the intermediate reasoning the judge uses to reach that final verdict.

**Practical Life Example for Visualisation**

Think about your ongoing case regarding the **leaking LG AC unit**.

* The **Fact in Issue** is: "Is the AC unit defective?" and "Did LG fail to provide proper service?" These are the main questions the Consumer Court must answer to decide if you get your money back.  
* The **Relevant Facts** would be: The technician’s visit reports, the emails you sent to customer service, and the photos of the water leak. These are not the "dispute" themselves, but they are "relevant" because they prove the defect exists.

**Conclusion**

Understanding the boundary between these two is vital. While the **Fact in Issue** forms the skeleton of the case, the **Relevant Facts** provide the flesh and blood that allow the court to see the whole picture. Without a fact in issue, there is no trial; without relevant facts, there is no way to reach a fair verdict.

### Question 2: 'Relevancy and admissibility are **not** co-extensive terms'. Comment in reference to the provisions of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 (now replaced by the Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023).

**Meaning of Co‑extensive**

Co‑extensive means covering the same scope or range.

If two things are co‑extensive, they are exactly equal in extent, application, or coverage.

**“Not co‑extensive” here means:**

* Relevancy is like a big circle (all logically connected facts).  
* Admissibility is a smaller circle inside it (only those relevant facts the law allows).

**Introduction**

The statement that "relevancy and admissibility are not co-extensive terms" is a fundamental principle of the law of evidence. In simple terms, it means that while a fact might be logically connected to a case (relevant), it may not necessarily be allowed into the courtroom record by the judge (admissible). Conversely, every fact that a judge admits into the record must be legally relevant. Under the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, which replaces the Indian Evidence Act (IEA), these two concepts function as the "two-step filter" for all evidence.

**The Theory of Relevancy**

Relevancy is based on logic and human experience. A fact is relevant when it has a certain degree of probative value—meaning it helps to prove or disprove the existence of a "fact in issue." Under the **BSA, Sections 4 to 50** (formerly Sections 5 to 55 of the IEA) define what the law considers "legally relevant."

The law of evidence is more restrictive than pure logic. For example, if a neighbor tells you they heard someone else say that 'A' stole a car, that might be "logically" relevant to your curiosity. However, in a court of law, it is only relevant if it fits into specific categories like **Section 4 (Res Gestae)** or **Section 26 (Dying Declarations)**. If it doesn't fit a specific section, it is considered legally irrelevant.

**The Theory of Admissibility**

Admissibility is a strictly legal concept. It is the "gatekeeper" stage. Even if a fact is logically and legally relevant under Sections 4 to 50, the Court may refuse to admit it for reasons of public policy, safety, or fairness. **Section 141** of the BSA (formerly Section 136 of the IEA) gives the Judge the power to decide whether a fact is admissible. If a party proposes to give evidence of a fact, the Judge asks how it is relevant. If the Judge is satisfied, they admit it; if there is a legal bar (like the rule against hearsay or privileged communication), they reject it.

**Why They Are “Not” Co-extensive**

The terms are **not** co-extensive because their boundaries do not overlap perfectly. The relationship can be understood through two major rules:

**1\. Facts that are Relevant but Inadmissible:** There are many facts that are highly relevant to finding the truth but are excluded by law.

* **Confessions to Police:** Under **Section 23** of the BSA, a confession made to a police officer is relevant to the guilt of the accused, but it is **inadmissible** to prevent police torture.  
* **Hearsay Evidence:** A statement made by a person who is not in court is relevant, but under **Section 55**, it is generally **inadmissible** because it cannot be cross-examined.  
* **Privileged Communications:** A conversation between a husband and wife or a lawyer and client is relevant, but under **Sections 128 and 132**, it is **inadmissible** to protect the sanctity of those relationships.

**2\. Facts that are Admissible but have little Relevancy:** Sometimes, for procedural reasons, the court admits facts that don't directly prove the crime but are necessary for the trial process. For example, the **name** and **address** of a witness are admissible, even though they don't prove the murder itself.

| **Basis** | **Relevancy** | **Admissibility** |
| **Source** | Based on logic and connection.  | Based on strict rules of law. |
| **Provision** | Sections 4 to 50 of BSA. | Determined by the Judge under Section 141\. |
| **Effect** | It is the "Genus" (broad). | It is the "Species" (narrow). |
| **Question of Law/Fact** | Mostly a question of fact and logic. | Purely a question of law.  |

**Practical Life Example for Visualisation**

Imagine your current dispute regarding the **leaking LG AC unit**.

* **Scenario A (Relevant but Inadmissible):** Suppose a former LG employee tells you in a private phone call, "We knew that specific model had a manufacturing defect." This is **highly relevant** to your case. however, if that employee refuses to come to court and you just try to tell the judge what they said, it becomes **inadmissible hearsay**. The "truth" is relevant, but the "method" of bringing it is legally barred.  
* **Scenario B (The Filter):** You show the judge a photo of the leaking unit. The judge first asks: "Is this photo of the same AC mentioned in the bill?" (**Step 1: Relevancy**). If yes, the judge then checks if the photo is digitally authenticated according to **Section 63** of the BSA (**Step 2: Admissibility**). Only if both filters are passed does the photo become "evidence."

**Conclusion**

To conclude, relevancy and admissibility are two different stages of the evidentiary process. Relevancy is the "potential" of a fact to be evidence, while admissibility is the "legal permission" for that fact to be heard. As the legal maxim goes, "Nothing is admissible which is not relevant, but not everything which is relevant is admissible." For a law student, mastering this distinction is key to understanding why some truths are kept out of court while others are let in.

### Question 3: How “facts” which are necessary to explain or “introduce relevant facts” are relevant under Sec. 9 of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 (BSA Section \- 7)? Explain.

**Introduction to Section 7 of the Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**

While your question refers to Section 9 of the old Indian Evidence Act, under your new syllabus, this principle is now found in **Section 7** of the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023**. This section is often called the "explanatory and introductory" section. It acts like a bridge that helps the Court understand the context of a case. Most facts in a trial don't make sense in isolation; they need a background story or a "connective tissue" to be understood properly. Section 7 makes these background facts legally relevant.

**The Five Categories of Facts under Section 7**

👍Remember that this section covers five specific types of facts that the Court allows to be proved:

**1\. Facts necessary to explain or introduce a fact in issue or relevant fact:**

Some facts are like the "introductory scene" of a movie. Without them, the main plot is confusing.

* **Practical Example:** If you are suing **LG Electronics** for your faulty AC, a fact in issue is whether the AC is defective. However, the fact that you purchased it on a specific date from a specific dealer and that you have a warranty card are "introductory facts." They explain your relationship with the company and introduce the main dispute.

**2\. Facts which support or rebut an inference:**

If a piece of evidence suggests a certain conclusion (an inference), any fact that strengthens or weakens that conclusion is relevant.

* **Practical Example:** 'A' is accused of a crime and is seen running away from the scene. The "running away" suggests he is guilty. However, if 'A' proves that he had a sudden emergency at home or was running to catch a train, this fact "rebuts" the inference of guilt.

**3\. Facts which establish the identity of anything or person:**

This is one of the most important uses of this section, covering **Test Identification Parades (TIP)**. When the identity of a person or a stolen object is in question, the process used to identify them is relevant.

* **Practical Example:** A witness says they saw a man with a scar on his face commit a robbery. Later, at the police station, the witness identifies the accused in a lineup of ten men. The fact that the witness picked him out correctly is relevant under Section 7 to establish identity.

**4\. Facts which fix the time or place of the incident:**

The "When" and "Where" are crucial for any trial. Facts that help determine exactly when or where an event occurred are relevant.

* **Practical Example:** In your motorcycle accident case from 2024, the exact location of the crash and the time (daylight vs. night) are relevant because they help the Court determine visibility and traffic conditions.

**5\. Facts which show the relation of parties:**

The relationship between the people involved often explains their motives or why they acted in a certain way.

* **Practical Example:** If 'A' is accused of murdering 'B', the fact that they were partners in a business that recently went bankrupt is relevant as it shows their "relation" and provides a background for the dispute.

**The Doctrine of Test Identification Parade (TIP)**

TIP is the most practical application of this section. When a crime is committed by a stranger, the police conduct a TIP where the witness identifies the suspect among several similar-looking people.

* **Evidentiary Value:** TIP is not "substantive evidence" (meaning you cannot convict someone *only* on a TIP), but it is used to "corroborate" (support) the witness's statement in court. It proves that the witness's memory is consistent.

**Illustrations from the Adhiniyam**

* **Illustration (a):** The question is, whether a given document is the will of 'A'. The state of 'A’s' property and of his family at the date of the alleged will may be relevant facts. (This explains the "context" of why he would write such a will).  
* **Illustration (b):** 'A' sues 'B' for a libel (defamation) imputing disgraceful conduct to 'A'; 'B' affirms that the matter alleged to be libellous is true. “What I wrote is not defamatory because it is true”. The position and relations of the parties at the time when the libel was published may be relevant facts as introductory to the facts in issue.

**Conclusion**

Section 7 of the BSA (Old Section 9 IEA) ensures that a trial is not just a collection of dry facts but a coherent story. It allows the Court to look at the "surrounding circumstances" that make the main evidence understandable. Whether it is identifying a suspect through a TIP or explaining a party's motive through their past relationship, this section provides the necessary framework for judicial reasoning.

### Question 4: "Anything said or done or written by any one of the conspirators in reference to their common intention as relevant fact not only against each of the conspirator but proving the conspiracy itself." Explain with case laws.

**Introduction to Section 8 of the Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**

This principle is found in **Section 8** of the BSA (which replaces Section 10 of the old Indian Evidence Act). It is a unique and powerful provision in criminal law. Generally, a person is only responsible for their own words or actions. However, in a conspiracy, the law treats all participants as "agents" of one another. This means that if three people agree to commit a crime, the words spoken by person A can be used as evidence against person B and person C, even if B and C weren't present when the words were spoken.

**The Doctrine of Agency**

The logic behind this section is that a conspiracy is like a partnership in crime. Just as a business partner can bind other partners through their actions, a conspirator binds their co-conspirators. The law assumes that once a "common intention" is formed, every act done by any member to further that goal is an act done on behalf of the whole group.

**Essential Conditions for Relevancy**

For a statement or action to be used against all members of a conspiracy under **Section 8**, four conditions must be met:

**1\. Reasonable Ground to Believe:** There must be some initial evidence (prima facie) to suggest that a conspiracy exists. The section cannot be used to *start* the proof of conspiracy; it is used once the court is satisfied that a conspiracy is likely.

**2\. Common Intention:** The act or statement must be "in reference to their common intention." It must be connected to the plan they all agreed upon.

**3\. Timing:** The act or statement must have happened **after** the intention was first entertained by any one of them.

**4\. Proving Existence and Participation:** Such evidence is relevant for two purposes: to prove that a conspiracy actually existed and to prove that a specific person was a member of it.

**Practical Life Example for Visualisation**

Imagine three people—**A, B, and C**—conspire to rob a bank.

* **A** buys the getaway car.  
* **B** writes a letter to a weapon dealer asking for guns "for our big project next Tuesday."  
* **C** is the one who actually enters the bank.  
  If the police find **B’s** letter, it can be used as evidence against **A** and **C** in court, even though **A** and **C** never saw the letter. Why? Because **B** was acting as their agent in the "common intention" of the robbery.

**Essential Case Laws**

To explain this concept fully, we must look at how the Courts have interpreted the phrase "in reference to their common intention."

**1\. Mirza Akbar v. King Emperor (1940):** This is the most famous case on this subject. In this case, a woman and her lover conspired to kill her husband. After the husband was killed and the lover was arrested, the woman wrote a letter to the lover expressing her feelings and discussing what happened. The Privy Council held that this letter was **not admissible** against the lover.

**The Rule:** The Court clarified that Section 10, IEA (new section 8 BSA) only applies to things said or done **while the conspiracy is still active**. Once the crime is committed or the conspiracy fails, any statement made after that (like a confession or a letter describing past events) is only evidence against the person who said it, not the others. It must be an act "in furtherance" of the plan, not just a "narrative" of what happened. 

It established that post-facto statements do not fall under the purview of Section 10\.

**2\. Badri Rai v. State of Bihar (1958):**

In this case, an accused person offered a bribe to a police officer to help his friend who was already in custody for another crime. The Supreme Court held that the act of offering the bribe was part of a conspiracy to suppress evidence.

**The Rule:** The Court ruled that the statement made by the person offering the bribe was admissible against his friend (the co-conspirator) because it was made "in reference to" their common objective of getting the friend out of legal trouble.

**3\. State (NCT of Delhi) v. Navjot Sandhu (2005) \- The Parliament Attack Case:**

The Supreme Court further refined this by stating that the words "in reference to" are very broad. However, they must be interpreted strictly. A communication between conspirators that is part of the execution of the plan is admissible. But a statement made to a third party that does not help the conspiracy move forward might be excluded.

**Conclusion**

**Section 8** of the BSA is a departure from the general rule of evidence to ensure that organized crimes do not go unpunished. Because conspiracies are hatched in secrecy, direct evidence is rare. This section allows the Court to piece together the "puzzle" by looking at the actions of all participants. However, as seen in **Mirza Akbar**, the law draws a strict line: the "agency" only exists while the plan is being carried out. Once the objective is achieved or the plan ends, the special rule of agency ends, and each person stands alone for their subsequent statements.

### Question 6\. When confession is relevant? State the principle of the use of a confession of an accused against a co-accused.

**Definition of Confession**

The term **confession** is not explicitly defined in the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, just as it wasn't in the old Evidence Act. However, it is judicially understood as a "species" of the "genus" **admission**. A confession is an admission made at any time by a person charged with a crime, stating or suggesting the inference that they committed that crime. For a statement to be a confession, it must either admit the offence in terms or substantially all the facts which constitute the offence.

**When is a Confession Relevant?**

Under the BSA, a confession is relevant and can be proved against the accused if it is made **voluntarily**. The law focuses on protecting the accused from coercion. A confession is relevant unless it falls under the following "irrelevant" categories:

**1\. Inducement, Threat, or Promise (Section 22):** A confession is irrelevant if it appears to the Court to have been caused by any inducement, threat, or promise relating to the charge, proceeding from a **person in authority** (like a police officer, magistrate, or employer). The inducement must be sufficient to give the accused grounds to think they would gain an advantage or avoid an evil of a temporal nature.

* **Practical Example:** If a police officer tells a suspect, "If you confess, I will make sure the judge gives you a lighter sentence," any resulting confession is irrelevant because it was caused by a promise from a person in authority.

**2\. Confession to a Police Officer (Section 23):** No confession made to a police officer shall be proved against a person accused of any offence. This is a strict rule to prevent custodial torture.

**3\. Confession in Police Custody (Section 23(2)):** A confession made while in police custody is irrelevant unless it is made in the **immediate presence of a Magistrate**.

* **The Discovery Exception (Section 23 Proviso):** If the accused in police custody gives information that leads to the **discovery of a fact** (like the location of a hidden weapon), then that specific part of the information is relevant, whether it was a confession or not.

**Principle of Using Confession Against a Co-Accused (Section 24\)**

The general rule of law is that a confession is only evidence against the person who makes it. However, **Section 24** of the BSA (corresponding to the old Section 30\) provides a major exception. It allows the Court to consider the confession of one accused against others under specific conditions.

**Essential Conditions for Section 24:**

* **Joint Trial:** The persons must be tried jointly for the **same offence**. If they are tried separately, the confession of one cannot be used against the other.  
* **Proved Confession:** The confession must be legally proved in court.  
* **Inculpatory Nature:** The confession must affect both the maker and the co-accused. A statement where the maker says "He did it, but I didn't" is not a confession under this section; the maker must implicate themselves as well.  
* **"May Take into Consideration":** This is a discretionary power. The confession of a co-accused is **not substantive evidence**. It is very weak evidence and can only be used to "lend assurance" to other independent evidence already on the record.

**The Explanation in BSA 2023:** A significant update in the **BSA** is **Explanation II to Section 24**, which clarifies that if a trial is held in the absence of an accused who has absconded, it is still deemed a **joint trial** for the purpose of this section.

**Practical Life Example for Visualisation**

Imagine two people, **Ravi and Amit**, are caught for a robbery and are being tried together. Ravi gives a voluntary judicial confession to the Magistrate saying, *"Amit and I planned the robbery and shared the money."*

Under **Section 24**, the Judge can use Ravi's confession to support the case against Amit. However, the Judge **cannot** convict Amit *only* on Ravi's word. There must be other evidence (like recovered jewelry or fingerprints). Ravi's confession only helps the Judge feel more "assured" about Amit's guilt.

**Conclusion**

Confessions under the **BSA 2023** are treated with great caution. The law ensures that only "free and voluntary" statements reach the court record. While **Section 24** allows a confession to spill over and affect a co-accused, it is treated as a "weak" type of evidence that requires strong corroboration from other sources to lead to a conviction.

### Question 7: Discuss the essential conditions for the relevancy of a dying declaration under Sec. 32(1) of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 (now Section 26(a) of the Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023).

While your question refers to Section 32(1) of the old Act, the governing provision in your new syllabus is **Section 26(a)** of the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**. A dying declaration is a statement made by a person as to the cause of their death or as to any of the circumstances of the transaction which resulted in their death. For such a statement to be legally relevant and admissible in a court of law, several essential conditions must be satisfied.

**1\. The Death of the Declarant**

The most fundamental condition is that the person who made the statement must be dead. If the person survives the injuries, the statement does not qualify as a dying declaration under Section 26(a). However, it may still be used to corroborate or contradict their oral testimony if they come to court as a witness.

**Practical Example:** If a person is stabbed and tells a doctor "Ravi stabbed me," but then recovers after surgery, that statement cannot be used as a dying declaration because the person is still alive to testify personally.

**2\. Statement must relate to the Cause of Death or Transaction resulting in Death**

The statement must specifically address how the person died or the circumstances that led to the fatal incident. Statements about distant past events or general gossip are not relevant under this section.

**Practical Example:** A statement by a woman to her mother that her husband has been harassing her for dowry for the last three months, followed by her death due to poisoning shortly after, is relevant as a "circumstance of the transaction" leading to death.

**3\. The Cause of Death must be "In Issue"**

The statement is only admissible in cases where the cause of that person's death comes into question. If the trial is about a different crime or person, the statement cannot be used.

**Practical Example:** In a trial for the murder of 'A', a statement made by 'B' (who is also dead) about how 'A' was killed is not a dying declaration under this section unless 'B’s' own death is also a subject of the same trial.

**4\. The Statement must be Complete and Consistent**

For a dying declaration to be reliable, it must not be incomplete. If a person begins to name their attacker but dies before finishing the sentence, the statement might be excluded if it creates ambiguity. It must also be voluntary and not the result of "tutoring" by relatives or police.

**5\. Competency and Fit State of Mind**

The declarant must have been in a "fit state of mind" to make the statement. This means they must have been conscious and mentally capable of understanding what they were saying. In practice, courts look for a medical certificate from a doctor testifying to the mental fitness of the deceased at the time of recording.

**Practical Example:** If a person is in a deep coma or semi-conscious and merely mumbles a name, the court may reject it unless a doctor confirms they were mentally alert enough to identify their attacker.

**6\. Expectation of Death (Not a Requirement in India)**

A very important point is that under the **BSA 2023**, it is **not necessary** that the person was in actual expectation of death when they made the statement. Even if they thought they would survive, if they eventually die from those injuries, the statement is relevant. This is a major departure from English Law, where the "expectation of death" is mandatory.

**7\. Form of the Declaration**

The law does not prescribe a specific format. A dying declaration can be:

* **Oral:** Spoken to a doctor, police officer, or relative.  
* **Written:** Recorded by a Magistrate or written by the victim themselves.  
* **Gestures:** If a person is unable to speak (e.g., throat is cut), they can point or nod in response to questions.

**Evidentiary Value**

While a dying declaration is a "weak" type of evidence because the maker cannot be cross-examined, the Supreme Court has held that if a dying declaration is found to be true and voluntary, it can form the sole basis of a conviction without any further corroboration. The Court follows the logic that a dying person is unlikely to lie before meeting their creator.

### Question 8: Explain under what circumstances secondary evidences are permissible in place of primary evidences.

The general rule of the law of evidence is that a document must be proved by its "Primary Evidence," which means the original document itself must be produced for the inspection of the Court. This is known as the **"Best Evidence Rule."** 

However, there are practical situations where the original document may be unavailable, lost, or impossible to bring to court. To prevent a miscarriage of justice in such cases, the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, allows "Secondary Evidence" (such as certified copies, photocopies, or oral accounts) to be used. **Section 60** of the BSA specifically lists the **seven circumstances** under which you can use secondary evidence.

**1\. When the original is in the possession of the opponent or an unreachable person** If the original document is with the person you are suing, or someone who is legally bound to give it to you but refuses to do so, you can provide secondary evidence. However, you must first give them a legal **"Notice to Produce"** under **Section 64**.

* **Practical Example:** You are suing **LG Electronics** for a faulty AC and they have the original service contract that you signed. You send them a notice to bring it to court. If they fail to produce it, you can show a photocopy of that contract as secondary evidence.

**2\. When the existence or contents are admitted in writing** If the person against whom you are proving the document has already admitted in writing that the document exists and has certain contents, that written admission is allowed as secondary evidence.

* **Practical Example:** You send an email to a dealer saying, "I have the receipt number 123 for the 50,000 rupees I paid." The dealer replies in an email, "Yes, we acknowledge receipt 123 for that amount." Even if you lose the actual paper receipt, the dealer's email is an admission that can be used as secondary evidence.

**3\. When the original is destroyed or lost** If the original document has been destroyed (e.g., in a fire) or lost, and it was not your own fault or neglect, you are permitted to give secondary evidence of its contents.

* **Practical Example:** Your house was flooded during a heavy monsoon in **Raebareli**, and your original property deeds were ruined. In a court case regarding that property, you can use a certified copy from the Registrar's office because the original is destroyed.

**4\. When the original is not easily movable** Sometimes the "document" is something that physically cannot be brought into a courtroom, such as an inscription on a heavy stone monument or a giant billboard.

* **Practical Example:** If you need to prove a warning sign was posted on a massive fixed steel structure at a construction site, the court will accept a photograph or a witness's description because the structure is not easily movable.

**5\. When the original is a Public Document** For documents that belong to the public record (like birth certificates, voter lists, or court judgments), the original stays in the government office. You prove these using **Certified Copies**.

* **Practical Example:** To prove your age in court, you don't need to bring the original massive ledger from the Municipal Corporation. A certified copy of your birth certificate issued by the officer is sufficient secondary evidence.

**6\. When a Certified Copy is permitted by law** There are certain documents where the law specifically states that a certified copy is enough evidence. In these cases, you do not need the original.

* **Practical Example:** Under the Registration Act, for any registered sale deed, the law allows you to use a certified copy issued by the Sub-Registrar as evidence of the original's contents.

**7\. Voluminous Documents and Numerous Accounts** If the original consists of so many accounts or papers that the Court cannot easily examine them all, a skilled person (like an accountant) can examine them and give a summary or the "general result" of the collection.

* **Practical Example:** In a massive corporate fraud case involving thousands of daily transaction logs, an auditor can testify to the "net loss" found after examining all the books, rather than the court reading every single page.

**Conclusion** While the law prefers the "Primary" original, **Section 60** of the BSA acts as a bridge for the truth. It ensures that a person's rights are not lost just because a piece of paper is missing or stuck in a government locker. 

👍Remember that for circumstances (a), (c), and (d), any type of secondary evidence is okay; but for (e) and (f), only a **certified copy** is allowed.

### Question 9: "The burden of proof lies on the party who substantially asserts the affirmative of the issue." Explain and mention its exceptions.

**🧠Affirmative of the issue** \= the positive claim in dispute.  
👍If you are the one saying *“X happened”*, you must prove it.

**The Concept of Burden of Proof** The "Burden of Proof" (BOP) is a fundamental principle of evidence law which dictates which party is responsible for proving a fact in a court of law. The quote in your question refers to the ancient legal maxim *ei incumbit probatio qui dicit, non qui negat*—meaning the proof lies upon him who affirms, not upon him who denies. Under the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, this concept ensures that the person who brings a case to court must back up their claims with evidence rather than expecting the other party to prove their innocence or non-liability.

**The General Rule (Section 104 and 105\)**

**Section 104** of the BSA states that whoever desires any Court to give judgment as to any legal right or liability dependent on the existence of facts which he asserts, must prove that those facts exist. When a person is bound to prove the existence of any fact, it is said that the burden of proof lies on that person. 

**Practical Example:** In your consumer dispute against **LG Electronics**, you are asserting that the air conditioner is faulty. Therefore, under Section 104, the burden is on you to prove the existence of that fault through technician reports or photos.

**Section 105** provides the "test" for identifying who carries this burden: the burden of proof in a suit or proceeding lies on that person who would fail if no evidence at all were given on either side. 

**Practical Example:** If you sue someone for a loan but neither of you brings any evidence to court, you (the plaintiff) will lose the case. Thus, the burden starts with you.

**Exceptions to the General Rule (When the Burden Shifts)**

While the primary burden often rests on the plaintiff or prosecution, the **BSA** provides several "exceptions" where the burden of proof shifts to the other party:

**1\. Burden of Proving a Particular Fact (Section 106):** The burden of proof as to any particular fact lies on the person who wishes the Court to believe in its existence. 

**Practical Example:** If a person is accused of theft but claims they were in another city at the time (an alibi), the burden of proving that they were in that other city lies on them, not the prosecution.

**2\. Case of Accused coming within Exceptions (Section 108):** In criminal trials, if an accused person claims their act falls under a "General Exception" (like insanity, accident, or self-defense), the burden of proving those circumstances is on the accused. The Court will initially presume that such circumstances did not exist. 

**Practical Example:** If an accused claims they committed a crime due to **unsoundness of mind**, they must prove they were incapable of knowing the nature of the act.

**3\. Fact Especially Within Knowledge (Section 109):** When any fact is especially within the knowledge of any person, the burden of proving that fact is upon them. 

**Practical Example:** If you are charged with traveling on a train without a ticket, the fact that you actually had a ticket is a fact "especially within your knowledge". Therefore, you must produce the ticket to prove you paid the fare.

**4\. Good Faith in Transactions of Active Confidence (Section 114):** Where there is a question as to the good faith of a transaction between parties where one stands in a position of "active confidence" (like a lawyer and client), the burden of proving the good faith of the transaction is on the party in the position of confidence. 

**Practical Example:** If a client sells property to their **advocate** and later claims they were cheated, the advocate must prove that the sale was fair and done in good faith.

**5\. Statutory Presumptions (Sections 117 and 118):** In specific cases like **Abetment of Suicide by a married woman** (within 7 years of marriage) or **Dowry Death**, the law shifts the burden. If the prosecution proves the woman was subjected to cruelty for dowry soon before her death, the Court **shall presume** the accused caused the death, and the burden shifts to the accused to prove otherwise.

**Conclusion**

The principle that the burden lies on the "affirmative" ensures that trials are fair and logical. It prevents parties from making baseless accusations without proof. However, through the exceptions in **Sections 106 to 118**, the **BSA** recognizes that sometimes one party is in a much better position to know the truth than the other, and in those specific cases, the law shifts the responsibility of proof to ensure justice is served.

### Question 10: Write short notes on any two of the following: (a) Relevancy of electronic evidence, (b) Leading Questions and (c) Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023\.

**Note (a): Relevancy of Electronic Evidence**

In the modern digital age, a significant portion of human interactions and transactions occur electronically. Recognizing this reality, the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, has elevated the status of electronic and digital records to be at par with traditional physical documents. Under **Section 61**, the law explicitly states that no electronic or digital record shall be denied admissibility solely on the ground that it is in electronic form. Such records, subject to the safety conditions mentioned in **Section 63**, have the same legal effect, validity, and enforceability as any other paper document.

The core of relevancy and admissibility for electronic evidence lies in **Section 63**. Any information contained in an electronic record—whether it is printed on paper, stored in a pen drive, or kept in cloud storage—is deemed to be a "document" **if specific conditions are met**. These conditions ensure that the "computer output" is reliable:

* The computer or communication device must have been used regularly to store or process such information for regular activities.  
* The information must have been fed into the device in the ordinary course of those activities.  
* The device must have been operating properly during the material period so as not to affect the accuracy of the record.

A **mandatory requirement** for the admission of any electronic record in court is the submission of a **Certificate** under **Section 63(4)**. This certificate must identify the electronic record, describe the device used to produce it, and be signed by a person in charge of the device or the management of the relevant activities, as well as an **expert**. The format for this certificate is provided in the **Schedule** of the Adhiniyam.

**Practical Example:** Suppose you want to use a **WhatsApp chat** or an **email** as evidence in your consumer case against **LG Electronics**. Under the BSA, you do not need to bring the entire server to court. Instead, you can provide a printout or a digital copy of the chat/email accompanied by the Section 63(4) certificate confirming that the smartphone/laptop was working properly and the data was not tampered with.

**Note (b): Leading Questions**

**Section 146(1)** of the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, defines a **leading question** as any question which suggests the specific answer that the person putting it wishes or expects to receive. Essentially, these are "loaded" questions that can typically be answered with a simple "Yes" or "No," effectively leading the witness toward a particular conclusion.

**The general rule regarding when these questions can be asked is divided into three parts:**

* **Prohibition in Examination-in-Chief and Re-examination:** Under **Section 146(2)**, leading questions must not be asked in an **examination-in-chief** or a **re-examination** if the adverse party objects, unless the Court gives its permission. This is to ensure that the witness provides their own account of the facts rather than just agreeing with the lawyer's suggestions.  
* **Mandatory Permission by the Court:** According to **Section 146(3)**, the Court **shall permit** leading questions for matters that are **introductory**, **undisputed**, or which have already been **sufficiently proved** in the Court's opinion. This helps save judicial time on non-controversial details like the witness's name or address.  
* **Permission in Cross-examination:** **Section 146(4)** explicitly states that leading questions **may be asked** in **cross-examination**. This is allowed because the goal of cross-examination is to test the witness's veracity and "shake their credit," and leading questions are powerful tools for exposing inconsistencies.

**Practical Examples for Visualisation:**

* **Leading Question (Allowed only in Cross-examination):** *"Isn't it true that you were standing 50 feet away and couldn't see the driver's face clearly?"* This question is leading because it suggests a specific distance and a lack of clarity.  
* **Non-Leading Question (Required for Examination-in-Chief):** *"Where were you standing, and what did you observe regarding the driver?"* This allows the witness to provide their own spontaneous details.

**Conclusion**

The regulation of leading questions under the **BSA** is vital for a fair trial. It ensures that the primary evidence comes directly from the memory of the witness while providing advocates with the necessary freedom to challenge that evidence during cross-examination.

👍Remember that the core purpose is to prevent "tutoring" of witnesses while allowing for a rigorous search for the truth.

**Note (c): Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023**

The **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, is a landmark piece of legislation that seeks to consolidate and provide general rules and principles of evidence for a fair trial in India. It received the assent of the President of India on **December 25, 2023**, and marks the end of the colonial-era Indian Evidence Act of 1872, which was formally repealed by **Section 170** of the new Adhiniyam.

The primary objective of the BSA is to **modernize the law of evidence** to keep pace with technological advancements and the changing nature of crime. Key highlights of this new legislation include:

* **Expanded Definitions:** The definition of "Document" now explicitly includes electronic and digital records, such as server logs, emails, locational evidence, and voicemail messages stored on digital devices. Similarly, "Evidence" now includes statements given electronically.  
* **Technological Neutrality:** By providing detailed procedures for the admission of digital records (Sections 61–63), the BSA ensures that digital evidence is treated with the same weight as physical evidence.  
* **Structural Continuity with Modernization:** While the BSA retains many foundational principles of the 1872 Act—such as the rules on Relevancy, Fact in Issue, and Burden of Proof—it reorders them and updates the language to reflect contemporary Indian legal thought.  
* **Secondary Evidence:** The BSA expands the scope of secondary evidence, especially for digital records, allowing for more flexibility when original documents are unavailable.

The BSA represents a shift toward a more robust, digitally-aware judicial system. It applies to all judicial proceedings before any Court, including Courts-martial, though it does not apply to affidavits or arbitration proceedings.

**Conclusion of Short Notes**

The **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023**, is not just a change in name but a fundamental update to how truth is determined in Indian courts. By integrating **electronic evidence** into the core of the law and providing clear safeguards for its use, the BSA ensures that the legal system remains relevant in a digital world while upholding the timeless principles of a fair trial.

## Question 1: State the law relating to 'Confession' made by an accused person as enunciated under Section 24 to 27 of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 (now Sections 22 and 23 of BSA, 2023).

**Introduction and Definition** 

A confession is a statement made by a person charged with a crime which suggests an inference that they committed the offence. Although the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, does not explicitly define "confession," it is legally treated as a "self-incriminating admission". The law surrounding confessions is designed to balance the need for discovering the truth with the protection of an accused person's fundamental rights against **self-incrimination** and **custodial torture**. 

Note that the principles previously found in Sections 24 to 27 of the Indian Evidence Act are now consolidated within **Sections 22 and 23** of the BSA.

1. **Confessions Caused by Inducement, Threat, or Promise (Section 22 BSA / Section 24 IEA)** 

Under **Section 22** of the BSA, a confession made by an accused is irrelevant in a criminal proceeding if the Court believes it was caused by any **inducement, threat, coercion, or promise**. For this rule to apply, the following must be true:

* The inducement or threat must come from a **person in authority** (such as a police officer, magistrate, or even a complainant).  
* It must relate to the charge against the accused.  
* It must give the accused a reasonable ground to believe they would gain a worldly advantage (like a lighter sentence) or avoid a worldly evil by confessing.   
  **Practical Example:** If a village Pradhan (a person in authority) tells an accused, "Confess to the theft, and I will make sure the police don't beat you," the resulting confession is irrelevant because it was obtained through a promise of safety.

2. **Confessions to Police Officers (Section 23(1) BSA / Section 25 IEA)** 

The law maintains a strict "no-confession" rule regarding the police. **Section 23(1)** of the BSA states that **no confession made to a police officer** shall be proved against a person accused of an offence. This is a protective shield to prevent the police from using physical or mental pressure to extract "ready-made" confessions. This rule is absolute; even if a confession to a police officer is completely voluntary, the Court will not admit it as evidence. 

**Practical Example:** If 'A' walks into a police station and says, "I killed my neighbor," that statement cannot be used in court as a confession. The prosecution must find independent evidence to prove the crime.

3. **Confessions in Police Custody (Section 23(2) BSA / Section 26 IEA)** 

A confession made while an accused is in **police custody** is also generally irrelevant under **Section 23(2)**. The only exception to this rule is if the confession is made in the **immediate presence of a Magistrate**. The presence of a judicial officer ensures that the accused is speaking freely and has not been threatened by the officers holding them.

4. **The "Discovery" Exception: The Doctrine of Confirmation (Section 23 Proviso BSA / Section 27 IEA)** 

The most significant part of this law is the **proviso to Section 23** (formerly Section 27 IEA), which acts as a major exception to the rules mentioned above. It states that when an accused in police custody provides information that leads to the **discovery of a fact**, so much of that information—whether it amounts to a confession or not—as relates distinctly to the fact discovered, may be proved. This is based on the "theory of confirmation by subsequent facts"; the fact discovered (like a hidden weapon) proves that the information provided was likely true. 

**Essential Conditions for Discovery:**

* The person must be **accused of an offence**.  
* The person must be in **police custody**.  
* The information must lead to the **discovery of a physical object** or a hidden fact.  
* Only the part of the statement that **relates distinctly** to the discovery is admissible.   
  **Practical Example:** 'A' is in custody and tells the police, "I killed 'B' with a knife and I have hidden that knife under a stone in my garden." Under Section 23, the part "I killed 'B'" is a confession and is excluded. However, the part "I have hidden that knife under a stone in my garden," followed by the police actually finding the knife there, is **admissible** in court because it led to a discovery.

**Conclusion**

The law relating to confessions under the BSA 2023 (and previously IEA) follows a logical hierarchy. It starts with a broad rejection of all coerced confessions (Section 22\) and confessions to police (Section 23), but then allows for a narrow "silver lining" where truth is confirmed by physical discovery (Proviso to Section 23).

## Question 2: Define the term 'Res Gestae' with essential conditions and essential elements of this principle.

The term **Res Gestae** is a Latin phrase that literally translates to "things done" or "part of the transaction". 

In the law of evidence, it refers to the facts, statements, and acts that are so closely connected to a main event that they are considered a part of it. 

Under the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, this principle is embodied in **Section 4**, which states that facts which are not in issue are still relevant if they are so connected with a fact in issue or a relevant fact as to form part of the **same transaction**. This rule is a major exception to the "hearsay rule," allowing the court to hear about spontaneous words or actions that occurred during a crime or event, even if the person who said them isn't in court.

For a fact to be relevant under the principle of **Res Gestae**, several **essential conditions** must be satisfied:

1. **Proximity of Time and Place:** While the law allows facts to occur at different times and places, there must be a continuous link. The statement or act must happen **simultaneously** with the main event or so shortly before or after it that there was no time for the person to manufacture a lie or "concoct" a story. If there is a significant time gap, the statement becomes a mere "narrative" of a past event and is no longer part of the **Res Gestae**.  
2. **Continuity of Action:** There must be a continuous thread of events. For example, if a person is being chased and they shout for help at various points during the chase, all those shouts are part of the same transaction because the action is continuous.  
3. **Community of Purpose:** The different acts or statements should be directed toward the same goal or result. In a conspiracy or a riot, multiple people doing different things in different locations are still part of the same transaction if they share a common objective.

The **essential elements** of this principle include the following types of facts:

1. **Spontaneous Statements:** Words shouted by the victim, the accused, or even **bystanders** during the incident are relevant. The key is spontaneity; the words must be a natural reaction to the event.  
2. **Acts and Omissions:** Physical actions done during the transaction, such as breaking a door or running away, are relevant. Even an **omission** (failing to do something) can be part of the **Res Gestae** if it happens during the transaction.  
3. **Circumstances of the Event:** Facts that show the state of things under which the event happened or the opportunity for it to occur are considered part of the transaction context.

**Practical Life Examples for Visualisation:**

**The Road Accident:** Imagine you are standing at a busy intersection in **Raebareli** and witness a car hit a pedestrian. You hear the driver scream, "Oh no, my brakes failed\!" and a bystander yells, "He was overspeeding\!". Even if you are the only one who comes to court, you can testify about what the driver and the bystander said. Those statements are part of the **Res Gestae** because they were made exactly when the accident happened.

**The Armed Insurrection:** If a group of people plans a rebellion and they attack a police station in one part of the city while another group burns a government bus in another part, both acts are relevant against all participants. Even if an accused person was only present at the bus burning, the attack on the police station is relevant because it forms part of the **"general transaction"** of the rebellion.

**The Libel Correspondence:** If a business rival sends you a series of letters, and the third letter contains a defamatory statement, the first two letters—even if they are polite—are relevant. They provide the necessary context and form part of the same "correspondence transaction".

In conclusion, the principle of **Res Gestae** under **Section 4** of the BSA ensures that the Court sees the "whole picture" of an event. By admitting facts that are logically and physically intertwined with the main issue, the law prevents the truth from being hidden behind technicalities. For your exam, Prateek, remember that the "same transaction" is the heart of this section, and it covers everything from the victim's cry to the bystander's reaction.

## Question 3: Explain the relevancy of motive, preparation and previous or subsequent conduct as given under the Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023\.

In the law of evidence, a person's psychological state and physical actions surrounding an event often provide the most reliable clues about their involvement. Under the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, these elements are governed by **Section 6** (which replaces Section 8 of the old Indian Evidence Act). This section makes three specific types of facts relevant: 

1. **Motive**,   
2. **Preparation**, and   
3. **Conduct**.

**The Relevancy of Motive (Section 6(1))** A motive is the "moving power" or the psychological reason that impels a person to do an act. While the prosecution is not legally required to prove motive to get a conviction, showing a strong motive makes it highly probable that the accused committed the crime. Under Section 6(1), any fact that shows or constitutes a motive for a fact in issue or relevant fact is itself relevant.

* **Practical Example:** If 'A' is tried for the murder of 'B', the fact that 'B' knew 'A' had committed a previous murder and was blackmailing 'A' to keep quiet is relevant. This provides a "motive" for 'A' to eliminate 'B'. (hatyare insaan ka hatya karne ka man karta hai).

**The Relevancy of Preparation (Section 6(1))** Preparation consists of devising or arranging the means or measures necessary for the commission of a crime. Acts of preparation are relevant because they show a planned intention. While preparing for a crime is generally not a crime itself (except in cases like waging war), evidence of such preparation is highly relevant in court to connect the accused to the eventual act.

* **Practical Example:** If 'A' is accused of poisoning 'B', the fact that 'A' was seen purchasing a specific type of rare poison just two days before 'B' died is relevant as "preparation". Similarly, if you are pursuing your case against **LG Electronics** for a faulty AC, the fact that you saved all your repair requests and photos of the leak serves as "preparation" for your legal claim.

**The Relevancy of Conduct (Section 6(2))** Conduct refers to the physical behavior of the parties involved in a case. Section 6(2) makes the conduct of any party (or their agent) relevant if it is influenced by or influences a fact in issue. This includes both **previous conduct** (before the event) and **subsequent conduct** (after the event).

* **Previous Conduct Example:** 'A' is accused of a crime. The fact that 'A' was seen scouting the victim's house every night for a week before the incident is relevant previous conduct.  
* **Subsequent Conduct Example:** After a robbery occurs, 'C' says in 'A’s' presence, "the police are coming to look for the person who robbed the bank," and 'A' immediately runs away. 'A’s' act of running away is a relevant subsequent conduct.

**The "Statements vs. Conduct" Rule (Explanations 1 & 2\)**

The law makes a clear distinction between what a person *does* and what they merely *say*:

* **Rule of Action:** According to **Explanation 1**, the word "conduct" does not include statements unless those statements accompany and explain an act. For example, if 'A' runs away (an act) shouting "I am innocent\!", the shout is relevant because it explains the act of running.  
* **Statements that Affect Conduct:** Under **Explanation 2**, if a statement is made *to* a person or in their presence, and that statement causes them to change their behavior, both the statement and the resulting conduct are relevant.  
* **Complaint vs. Statement:** The BSA makes a vital distinction in rape or robbery cases. If a victim makes a formal **complaint** shortly after the crime, it is relevant conduct. However, if they merely tell a friend "I was raped/robbed" without the intention of seeking legal redress, it is not relevant as "conduct" under Section 6 (though it might be a dying declaration under Section 26).

**Conclusion**

Section 6 of the BSA ensures that a trial is not just about the final act but about the entire "behavioral chain." By allowing evidence of motive, preparation, and conduct, the law allows the Court to look into the mind and actions of the parties to separate the guilty from the innocent.

👍 Remember that while motive and preparation happen *before* the crime, conduct can happen both *before and after*, and it is the physical reaction to the event that carries the most weight in court.

## Question 4: What is Dying Declaration? Evaluate the **significance** of dying declaration.

**Definition of Dying Declaration** Under the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, a dying declaration is governed by **Section 26(a)**. It is a statement, either written or verbal, made by a person who is now dead, regarding the cause of their death or the circumstances of the transaction that resulted in their death. This statement becomes relevant in cases where the cause of that person's death is a "fact in issue" before the Court.

A distinctive feature of the law in India (continued in the BSA) is that such a statement is relevant whether or not the person was under an "expectation of death" at the time they made it. Furthermore, it is admissible in both civil and criminal proceedings where the cause of death is being questioned.

**The Rationale: Why is it Admitted?**

The admission of a dying declaration is an exception to the general rule that "hearsay evidence is no evidence." Its legal validity is based on two major principles:

1. **Nemo moriturus praesumitur mentiri:** This Latin maxim means "a man will not meet his Maker with a lie in his mouth." The law presumes that the solemnity of approaching death removes all motives for falsehood.  
2. **Principle of Necessity:** In many cases of homicide, the victim is the only eyewitness. Excluding their statement would mean allowing a murderer to go free simply because the primary witness is no longer alive to testify.

**Essential Conditions for a Valid Dying Declaration**

For a statement to be admissible under Section 26(a) of the BSA, the following conditions must be met:

* **The Declarant must be Dead:** The person who made the statement must have died before the statement is offered as evidence. If the person survives, it is not a dying declaration but can be used as a witness statement to corroborate or contradict their testimony.  
* **Cause of Death or Circumstances of Transaction:** The statement must relate specifically to how the person died or the events leading directly to their death. Statements about unrelated past events are not covered.  
* **Fit State of Mind:** The person must have been mentally capable of understanding and making the statement. Courts usually require a "fit to make statement" certificate from a doctor if the declaration was recorded in a hospital.  
* **Completeness:** While a dying declaration does not need to be a long narrative, it should not be an incomplete thought that leaves the identity of the attacker in doubt.

**⭐ Evaluating the Significance (Evidentiary Value)**

The significance of a dying declaration is immense, but it is handled with "judicial caution." Its significance can be evaluated as follows:

1. **Substantive Evidence:** A dying declaration is considered substantive evidence. This means the Court can rely on it as a **primary source of truth** to determine the guilt of the accused.  
2. **Sole Basis of Conviction:** The Supreme Court of India has repeatedly held that a conviction can be based **solely** on a dying declaration without any other corroboration, provided the Court is satisfied that the declaration is true, voluntary, and not the result of "tutoring" by relatives or police.  
3. **Consultative Nature:** If there are multiple dying declarations that contradict each other, their significance decreases. In such cases, the Court looks for independent evidence (like forensic reports or other witnesses) to decide which version is true.  
4. **Superiority of Recorded Versions:** A declaration recorded by a **Magistrate** carries the highest significance. Statements made to doctors or police officers are also important but may face stricter scrutiny. Oral statements made to relatives are considered the "weakest" form of dying declaration.

**Practical Examples and Illustrations**

**Example 1: The "Direct Cause"** A woman is brought to the hospital with 80% burn injuries. She tells the attending doctor, "My husband and mother-in-law poured kerosene on me because I didn't bring enough dowry." She dies two hours later. This is a classic dying declaration. Its significance is high because it directly names the attackers and the motive.

**Example 2: The "Transaction" (Illustration (a) from BSA)** The question is, whether A was murdered by B; or A dies of injuries received in a transaction in the course of which she was raped. The question is whether she was raped by B; or whether A was killed by B under such circumstances that a suit would lie against B by A's widow. Statements made by A as to the cause of his or her death, referring respectively to the murder, the rape and the actionable wrong under consideration, are relevant facts.

**Example 3: Non-Verbal Signs**

In the famous *Nirbhaya* case, the victim was unable to speak due to her injuries but made gestures and signs in response to questions by a Magistrate. The Court held that **gestures and signs** are valid forms of a dying declaration and used them to convict the accused.

**Conclusion** The dying declaration is a powerful tool for justice, especially in crimes committed behind closed doors like dowry deaths or secret assaults. While it lacks the safeguard of cross-examination, its significance is preserved by the high standard of "judicial conscience" applied by the Judge. For your exam, remember that **Section 26(a)** of the BSA ensures that the final words of a victim are heard in the halls of justice.

## Question 5: Elaborate the principle of estoppel as contained in Section 115 of the Indian Evidence Act (now Section 121 of BSA, 2023), giving illustrations. What is estoppel against a statute? Can such an estoppel be pleaded?

**The Principle of Estoppel**

The doctrine of **Estoppel** is a rule of equity and law that prevents a person from taking inconsistent positions in a legal proceeding. Its primary purpose is to ensure fairness and prevent a party from profiting from a false representation that another party has relied upon. Under the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, this principle is found in **Section 121** (formerly Section 115 of the IEA).

**Section 121** defines Estoppel as: When one person has, by his **declaration, act or omission**, intentionally caused or permitted another person to believe a thing to be true and to act upon such belief, neither he nor his representative shall be allowed, in any suit or proceeding between himself and such person or his representative, to deny the truth of that thing.

**Essential Ingredients of Estoppel**

For the principle of estoppel to apply, the following three conditions must be met:

1. **Representation:** There must be a representation made by one person to another through words (declaration), physical actions (act), or silence when there was a duty to speak (omission).  
2. **Intent and Belief:** The person making the representation must have **intentionally** caused the other person to believe that representation to be true.  
3. **Action in Reliance:** The other person must have **acted upon that belief** and altered their position. If they knew the truth or did not change their behavior because of the statement, estoppel cannot be claimed.

**Illustrations of Estoppel**

**Statutory Illustration:** 'A' intentionally and falsely leads 'B' to believe that a certain piece of land belongs to 'A', inducing 'B' to buy and pay for it. Later, 'A' actually inherits the land and becomes its true owner. 'A' then tries to cancel the sale to 'B', claiming he had no title at the time of the sale. Under **Section 121**, 'A' is **estopped** and will not be allowed to prove his lack of title to invalidate the sale.

**Practical Example:** Suppose you are trying to rent out your commercial hall in **Raebareli**. If you tell a potential tenant that the hall is 1,200 sq. ft. when it is actually 1,000 sq. ft., and they sign the lease and pay a deposit based on that "fact," you cannot later refuse to honor the lease terms by claiming the size was a mistake once you find a better-paying tenant. You are **estopped** from denying your own statement because the tenant acted upon it.

**Estoppel Against a Statute**

The phrase **"estoppel against a statute"** refers to an attempt to use the doctrine of estoppel to override or bypass a mandatory requirement of the law. For instance, if a law requires a specific contract to be registered for it to be valid, a party cannot use estoppel to claim that the contract is valid without registration just because the other party promised it wouldn't be necessary.

**Can Estoppel Against a Statute be Pleaded?**

The well-settled rule in Indian law is that there is **no estoppel against a statute**. It **cannot be pleaded** for the following reasons:

* **Supreme Authority of Law:** A statute represents the will of the legislature and public policy. Private representations or agreements between individuals cannot be allowed to defeat the mandatory provisions of the law.  
* **Public Duty:** Estoppel cannot be used to prevent a government body or a public official from performing a duty imposed on them by a statute.  
* **Illegal Acts:** The law will not enforce an act that is statutorily illegal or void simply because one party misled another. For example, a minor (under 18\) who fraudulently claims to be an adult to enter a contract is not "estopped" from pleading their minority because the law (Contract Act) specifically protects minors.

**Conclusion**

While **Estoppel** is a powerful tool to ensure honesty and consistency, it is secondary to the **law of the land**. It works to stop a person from denying a **fact** they asserted, but it cannot be used to change or ignore a **legal requirement**.

## Question 6: Who is an accomplice? Discuss the rules applicable to the admissibility of the testimony of an accomplice.

**Definition of an Accomplice**

An **accomplice** is a person who is a "guilty associate in crime." 

They are individuals who have participated in the commission of an offence along with the accused and are, therefore, liable to be charged for the same crime. In legal proceedings, an accomplice often turns into an "approver"—someone who is granted a pardon by the court on the condition that they make a full and true disclosure of the entire circumstances of the crime and every other person involved.

Accomplices are generally categorized into:

1. **Principals:** Those who actually commit the crime or are present aiding and abetting it.  
2. **Accessories before the fact:** Those who incite, counsel, or command the crime but are not present when it happens.  
3. **Accessories after the fact:** Those who, knowing a crime has been committed, help the offender escape or hide evidence.

**Admissibility and Competency under BSA, 2023**

The rules regarding the testimony of an accomplice under the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, are found in two distinct parts of the Act, which must be read together to understand the law correctly.

**1\. Accomplice as a Competent Witness (Section 138):**

**Section 138** (replaces Section 133 of the old Evidence Act) explicitly states that an accomplice is a **competent witness** against an accused person. It further clarifies that a conviction is **not illegal** merely because it proceeds upon the uncorroborated testimony of an accomplice. This is the **Rule of Law**. It means that, technically, a Judge has the legal power to convict someone based solely on the word of a criminal partner.

**👍 Example for Section 138: The "Solitary Truth" (Rule of Law)**

Imagine two people, **Arjun and Bhavesh**, are accused of stealing a high-end laptop from a library. **Arjun** decides to confess and testifies, "Bhavesh and I climbed the window at 11 PM and took the laptop." During the trial, **Arjun** gives such a detailed, consistent, and convincing account that the Judge is 100% certain he is telling the truth, even though the police never found the laptop or any CCTV footage. Under **Section 138**, the Judge has the legal power to convict **Bhavesh** based *only* on **Arjun's** word. Even without "back-up" evidence, the conviction is **legally valid** because the law trusts the Judge’s ability to spot the truth.

**2\. Presumption of Unworthiness (Section 119, Illustration b):**

**Section 119** (replaces Section 114 of the old Evidence Act) allows the Court to presume the existence of certain facts. **Illustration (b)** states that the Court may presume **"that an accomplice is unworthy of credit, unless he is corroborated in material particulars."** This is the **Rule of Prudence**. It reflects the reality that an accomplice is a person of low character who might be lying to save their own skin or to please the police in exchange for a pardon.

**👍 Example for Section 119, Illustration (b): The "Suspicious Partner" (Rule of Prudence)** 

Now, imagine a different case where **Chetan** is accused of a multi-crore scam and his partner, **Deepak**, becomes an approver. **Deepak** says, "Chetan was the mastermind; I was just following orders." However, the Judge notices that **Deepak** has a history of fraud and is obviously trying to pin everything on **Chetan** to get a pardon. There are no bank records, no emails, and no other witnesses to support **Deepak's** story. Under **Section 119 (b)**, the Judge will act with **caution (prudence)** and presume that **Deepak** is "unworthy of credit." Unless the prosecution brings in a "material particular"—like a bank transfer slip showing **Chetan** receiving the stolen money—the Judge will likely acquit **Chetan**, refusing to trust a criminal's word blindly.

**The Rule of Corroboration**

Because these two sections seem to contradict each other (one says you *can* convict without support, the other says you *shouldn't*), the Higher Courts have established a middle ground. The established rule is that while a conviction is not "illegal" without corroboration, as a matter of **judicial caution**, a Court should almost never convict based solely on an accomplice's testimony.

For corroboration to be valid, it must meet three standards:

* **Independent Source:** The supporting evidence must come from someone other than the accomplice or another accomplice. One accomplice cannot corroborate another.  
* **Material Particulars:** The evidence doesn't need to prove the whole crime, but it must connect the accused with the crime in a significant way.  
* **Specific Identification:** The evidence must prove not just that a crime was committed, but that the **specific accused** person committed it.

**Practical Life Example for Visualisation**

Imagine three people—**A, B, and C**—rob a jewelry store. **A** is caught and agrees to be an "approver." He testifies that "B and C were with me, and B used a silver hammer to break the glass."

* Under **Section 138**, **A** is a competent witness.  
* However, under **Section 119**, the Judge will be suspicious of **A**.  
* If the police find a **silver hammer** in **B’s** house and a witness who saw **C** running away from the store, this is **corroboration in material particulars**. Now, the Judge can safely rely on **A’s** testimony to convict **B** and **C**.

\[Image showing the process of an accomplice becoming an approver and providing corroborated testimony\]

**Conclusion**

The law regarding accomplices is a "tightrope walk" for the judiciary. On one hand, the testimony of an accomplice is often the only way to break into secret criminal conspiracies. On the other hand, relying on a criminal's word is dangerous. By balancing **Section 138** (legality) with **Section 119** (prudence), the **BSA, 2023**, ensures that accomplice testimony is used as a "starting point" for the truth, but is only accepted as the "final word" when supported by independent facts.

## Question 7: What is documentary evidence? Discuss the law relating to relevancy of secondary evidence in legal proceedings.

**Definition of Documentary Evidence** 

Under **Section 2(1)(d)** of the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, a **"document"** is any matter expressed or described upon any substance by means of letters, figures, or marks, intended to be used for the purpose of recording that matter. **Documentary evidence** refers to all documents produced for the inspection of the Court. Remember that the BSA has significantly expanded this definition to include **electronic records**, such as emails, server logs, and smartphone messages.

**The Best Evidence Rule** 

The law generally demands the "best evidence," which is the **Primary Evidence** (the original document itself). However, when the original is unavailable, **Secondary Evidence** (copies or oral accounts) becomes relevant. Under the BSA, the rules for when you can skip the original and use a copy are found in **Sections 58 to 60**.

**Circumstances for Relevancy of Secondary Evidence** 

Secondary evidence is relevant and admissible in the following specific legal circumstances:

**1\. Original in Possession of the Adversary:** If you want to prove a document that is currently held by the person you are suing (like **LG Electronics** holding your original service contract), and they refuse to produce it after receiving a legal notice, you can use a photocopy as secondary evidence.

**2\. Existence Admitted in Writing:** If the other party has already signed a paper or sent an email admitting that the document exists and says X, Y, and Z, that admission is relevant secondary evidence.

**3\. Original Destroyed or Lost:** If the original was lost (and it wasn't your fault) or destroyed in a fire or flood, the Court allows you to prove the contents through a copy.

**4\. Original Cannot be Easily Moved:** If the "document" is an inscription on a massive rock or a fixed billboard, you don't have to bring the rock to court; a photograph or a witness's description is relevant.

**5\. Public Documents:** For documents like birth certificates or court orders, the "original" stays in a government locker. You prove these using **Certified Copies**.

**6\. Numerous Accounts:** If the evidence consists of thousands of pages of bank statements that the Judge cannot possibly read, a summary prepared by an expert (like a Chartered Accountant) is relevant secondary evidence of the "general result" of those accounts.

**Conclusion** 

Documentary evidence is the "silent witness" of the legal world. While **Primary Evidence** is the gold standard, the law on **Secondary Evidence** ensures that justice isn't blocked just because a piece of paper is missing or stuck in a government office. For your exams, keep in mind that with the **BSA 2023**, a properly authenticated digital copy is now treated with almost the same weight as a physical paper original.

## Question 8: "The art of cross-examination is a double-edged weapon". Critically comment. State what questions cannot be asked in cross-examinations.

The term **cross-examination** (Hindi word: **प्रति-परीक्षा** \- *Prati-pariksha*) is defined under **Section 141(2)** of the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**. It is the examination of a witness by the adverse party. It is widely considered the most powerful tool ever invented for the discovery of truth, but it is also famously described as a "double-edged weapon" because while it can destroy the opponent's case, a poorly handled cross-examination can end up destroying your own.

---

**Critically Comment: Why is it a "Double-Edged Weapon"?**

The metaphor of a "double-edged weapon" highlights the risk-reward nature of this legal art.

* **The Sharp Edge (The Benefit):** Cross-examination is used to "shake the credit" of a witness. It exposes lies, reveals biases, and tests the memory of the person on the stand. Under **Section 149** of the BSA, you can ask questions to test their veracity, discover who they are, and shake their credit by injuring their character. For example, if a witness in your LG case claims the AC was never leaking, your cross-examination can force them to admit they never actually visited the site, thereby proving their testimony is unreliable.  
* **The Blunt Edge (The Risk):** If a lawyer is not careful, they might ask a question that allows the witness to explain away a contradiction or, worse, reinforce their original story with more detail. A classic mistake is asking "Why?" which gives the witness an open door to justify their position. If you "cut" the witness with a sharp question but they have a strong answer, that same weapon "cuts" back at your own case by making the witness look even more credible to the Judge.

---

**Questions that Cannot be Asked in Cross-Examination**

While cross-examination is broad, it is not an "all-access pass" to harass a witness. The **BSA, 2023**, imposes strict boundaries on what is prohibited:

**1\. Questions without Reasonable Grounds (Section 152):**

A lawyer cannot ask a question that injures a witness's character unless they have "reasonable grounds" to believe the imputation is true. You cannot just guess that a witness is a thief to make them look bad; you must have a basis for that claim.

**2\. Indecent and Scandalous Questions (Section 154):**

The Court may forbid any questions which are indecent or scandalous, even if they have some bearing on the case, unless they relate to facts in issue or to matters necessary to be known to determine whether the facts in issue existed.

* **Practical Example:** In a property dispute, asking a witness about their private romantic life is generally forbidden as "scandalous" unless it directly proves a motive for fraud.

**3\. Questions Intended to Insult or Annoy (Section 155):**

The Court shall forbid any question which appears to be intended to insult or annoy the witness. Even if the question is technically proper, if it is asked in a needlessly offensive or "brow-beating" manner, the Judge will step in.

**4\. Prohibited Questions in Specific Sexual Offences (Section 149 Proviso):**

In prosecutions for rape or attempt to commit rape, it is strictly forbidden to ask questions or adduce evidence regarding the **victim's character** or their **previous sexual experience** with any person to prove consent or the quality of consent.

**5\. Questions on Privileged Communications:**

You cannot ask a witness to reveal "privileged" information, such as confidential talks between a husband and wife or a lawyer and client, unless they have waived that privilege.

---

**Conclusion**

The art of cross-examination requires a surgical balance of aggression and restraint. Remember that while **Section 146(4)** allows you to ask "leading questions" during cross-examination, **Sections 154 and 155** act as the "shield" protecting the witness from abuse. A great lawyer knows not just which questions to ask, but more importantly, which ones to leave unasked.

## Question 9: What do you mean by "privileged communications" as given under the Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023?

**Privileged Communications** (**विशेषाधिकृत संसूचना** — *Visheshadhikrit Sansuchna*) refers to certain confidential conversations or information that are protected by law from being disclosed in a court of law. The law recognizes that some relationships (like husband-wife or lawyer-client) are so sacred or vital to society that forcing a person to reveal what was said in private would cause more harm than good. Under the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, these protections are found in **Sections 128 to 135** (formerly Sections 122 to 129 of the IEA).

---

**1\. Matrimonial Privilege (Section 128\)**

**Hindi Word:** **वैवाहिक विशेषाधिकार** (*Vaivahik Visheshadhikar*)

This section states that no person who is or has been married shall be compelled to disclose any communication made to them during the marriage by their spouse. Even after divorce or the death of a spouse, the privilege continues for conversations that happened while they were married.

* **Exception:** This privilege does not apply in suits between the married persons themselves (like a divorce case) or in criminal proceedings for a crime committed by one spouse against the other.

**Example:** During their marriage, *Amit* tells his wife *Neha* that he secretly forged documents at work. Later, Amit is prosecuted for forgery. Neha cannot be compelled to testify about Amit’s confession, even after divorce.

**Exception:** If Neha files for divorce alleging cruelty, she can disclose communications relevant to that case.

**2\. Affairs of State (Section 129\)**

**Hindi Word:** **राज्य के कार्य** (*Rajya ke Karya*)

No one shall be permitted to give evidence derived from unpublished official records relating to any affairs of State, except with the permission of the head of the department concerned. The Court cannot compel the production of such documents if the department head believes it would be against the public interest.

**Example:** A journalist sues the government and demands unpublished defense strategy papers. The head of the Defense Ministry refuses, stating disclosure would harm national security. The Court cannot compel production without departmental permission.

**3\. Official Communications (Section 130\)**

**Hindi Word:** **शासकीय संसूचना** (*Shaskiya Sansuchna*)

A public officer cannot be compelled to disclose communications made to them in "official confidence" if they believe that the public interest would suffer by the disclosure.

**Example:** A District Collector receives a confidential report from a subordinate about corruption in a local project. In court, the Collector cannot be forced to reveal the contents if they believe disclosure would harm public interest.

**4\. Information as to Commission of Offences (Section 131\)**

**Hindi Word:** **अपराधों के किए जाने के बारे में जानकारी** (*Aparadhon ke kiye jaane ke baare mein jaankari*)

No Magistrate or police officer can be compelled to say where they got any information as to the commission of any offence. Similarly, a Revenue Officer cannot be compelled to say where they got information about an offence against the public revenue. This protects "informants" from retaliation.

**Example:** A police officer arrests a gangster based on a tip from a secret **मुखबिर** (*Mukhbir* \- Informant). In court, the gangster’s lawyer asks the police officer, "Who exactly gave you the tip? Give me his name and address." The Judge will stop this question. The law protects the identity of the informant so that people continue to help the police without fear of being killed by criminals.

**5\. Professional Communications (Section 132\)**

**Hindi Word:** **वृत्तिक संसूचना** (*Vrittik Sansuchna*)

An advocate (lawyer) is not permitted to disclose any communication made to them by their client in the course of and for the purpose of their employment. This is essential for a fair trial, as a client must be able to speak freely to their lawyer.

* **Exception:** This privilege does **not** protect communications made in furtherance of any illegal purpose or if the lawyer notices that a crime or fraud has been committed since their employment began.

**Example:**  

* *Rahul* consults his lawyer *Meera* and admits he evaded taxes. Meera cannot disclose this communication in court.

**Exception:**  

* If Rahul tells Meera he plans to forge new tax documents tomorrow, Meera is not bound by privilege because the communication is in furtherance of an illegal act.

---

**Summary of Exceptions**

Privileged communication is not absolute. It can be waived if:

* The person who is entitled to the privilege **consents** to the disclosure.  
* The communication was made for a **criminal or fraudulent purpose**.  
* The information is already **publicly known**.

**Conclusion**

The doctrine of privileged communication balances the search for truth in court with the need to protect private and professional trust. While the Court generally wants all facts, it respects these "zones of privacy" to maintain the stability of families, the efficiency of the government, and the integrity of the legal profession.

## Question 10: Write short notes on the following: (a) 'Fact' and 'Fact in Issue', (b) Expert opinion, (c) Burden of proof.

**Note (a): 'Fact' and 'Fact in Issue'** 

**Hindi Word for Fact:** **तथ्य** (*Tathya*) **Hindi Word for Fact in Issue:** **विवाद्यक तथ्य** (*Vivadhyak Tathya*)

Under **Section 2(1)(f)** of the **Bhartiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**, a **Fact** is not just a physical object but includes anything capable of being perceived by the senses, as well as any mental condition of which a person is conscious. For example, that you saw a red car is a physical fact; that you had the intention to buy it is a psychological fact.

A **Fact in Issue** (**Section 2(1)(g)**) is much narrower. It refers only to those facts that one party asserts (claims) and the other party denies in a legal proceeding. These are the "questions" the Judge must answer to decide the case. For example, in a murder trial, the fact that the accused held a knife is a **fact**, but whether he used that knife to kill the victim with "intention" is the **fact in issue**.

**Note (b): Expert Opinion** 

**Hindi Word:** **विशेषज्ञ की राय** (*Visheshagya ki Rai*)

Generally, witnesses are only allowed to testify about what they saw or heard (facts). However, under **Section 39** of the BSA, the Court can seek the opinion of an **Expert** on matters involving foreign law, science, art, finger impressions, or handwriting. An expert is someone who has special skill or experience in that specific field.

While the Judge is not "bound" to follow the expert's opinion, it is highly persuasive. For example, if a dead body is found, a **Doctor** (Medical Expert) provides an opinion on the time of death. Or, in a forgery case, a **Handwriting Expert** compares signatures to see if they match. Their testimony is relevant because the Judge cannot be an expert in every scientific field.

**Note (c): Burden of Proof** 

**Hindi Word:** **सबूत का भार** (*Saboot ka Bhar*)

This principle, found in **Section 104** of the BSA, determines which party is responsible for proving a fact in court. The general rule is **"He who asserts must prove."** If you bring a case to court, the burden is on you to provide evidence. If no evidence is given by either side, the person who brought the case will lose.

In criminal cases, the **Burden of Proof** is always on the prosecution to prove the accused's guilt "beyond a reasonable doubt." However, in some cases, the burden shifts. For example, under **Section 108**, if an accused claims they killed someone in "Self-Defence," the burden of proving that specific fact shifts to the accused.

⭐ **Standard of Proof: Preponderance of Probabilities** This is the biggest difference between civil and criminal law. In your criminal law studies, you know the standard is "Beyond Reasonable Doubt." In a civil case, the standard is the **Preponderance of Probabilities** (**संभावनाओं की प्रबलता** — *Sambhavnaon ki Prabalata*).

* In a criminal case, the evidence must be **99% to 100%** certain to convict.  
* In a civil case, the Judge simply asks: **"Is it more likely than not that this happened?"** If the plaintiff's story is **51%** believable and the defendant's is **49%**, the plaintiff wins.

## Question 1: What do you understand by relevancy of facts? Are all the relevant facts admissible in Court? Explain.

**Relevancy of Facts** (**तथ्यों की सुसंगति** — *Tathyon ki Susangati*) refers to the logical connection between two facts. A fact is "relevant" if it has the potential to prove or disprove the existence of a "fact in issue." Under the **BSA**, **Sections 4 to 50** define which facts the law considers relevant.

**Are all relevant facts admissible?** The answer is **No**. 

Relevancy is the "genus" (broad) and Admissibility is the "species" (narrow). A fact may be logically relevant but legally **inadmissible** (**अस्वीकार्य** — *Asveekarya*) due to specific legal bars or public policy.

* **Example 1:** A confession to a police officer is logically relevant to the guilt of the accused, but it is **inadmissible** under **Section 23** to prevent custodial torture.  
* **Example 2:** A private conversation between a husband and wife about a crime is relevant, but it is **inadmissible** under **Section 128** to protect marital sanctity.  
* **Conclusion:** Relevancy is based on logic; Admissibility is based on strict rules of law. As a law student, remember: **"All admissible facts are relevant, but not all relevant facts are admissible."**

---

## Question 5: Explain 'Admission' and 'Confession' and distinguish between them. Also discuss as to what extent admission and confession are admissible in the Court.

**Admission** (**स्वीकृति** — *Sveekriti*) is a statement (oral, documentary, or electronic) which suggests an inference as to any fact in issue or relevant fact (**Section 15 BSA**). It is usually used in civil cases.

**Section 16** (formerly Section 18 of the IEA) provides a clear list of five categories of people whose statements can be treated as admissions:

**1\. The Parties to the Proceeding:** This includes the plaintiff (**वादी** — *Vaadi*) and the defendant (**प्रतिवादी** — *Prativadi*). In your case against **LG Electronics**, any statement made by you or LG’s authorized representative in court counts as an admission.

**2\. Agents of the Parties:** Statements made by an agent (like a lawyer or a manager) whom the Court regards as expressly or impliedly authorized by the party to make them.

**3\. Parties Suing or Sued in a Representative Character:** If a person is suing on behalf of an estate or a minor, their statements are admissions only if they were made while they held that representative character.

**4\. Persons with Proprietary or Pecuniary Interest:** If someone has a financial or ownership interest in the subject matter of the suit, their statement can be used against the parties. For example, if you and a business partner jointly own that **commercial hall in Raebareli**, a statement made by your partner about the property could be an admission used against you.

**5\. Persons from whom Interest is Derived:** If you bought a piece of land from Mr. Sharma, any statement Mr. Sharma made about the land *while he owned it* can be used as an admission against you.

**Confession** (**संसुइकृति** — *Sansveekriti*) is a type of admission made by a person charged with a crime, admitting their guilt. It is used in criminal cases.

**Differences between Admission and Confession:**

| Feature | Admission (स्वीकृति) | Confession (संसुइकृति) |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Nature** | General term; applies to any fact. | Specific term; admits guilt of a crime. |
| **Proceedings** | Primarily used in civil cases. | Strictly used in criminal cases. |
| **Maker** | Can be made by a party, agent, or person with interest. | Only made by the person accused of the crime. |
| **Effect** | Acts as an estoppel; not always conclusive proof. | If voluntary, can be the sole basis for conviction. |
| **Form** | Can be made to anyone. | Must be voluntary; if to police, it is generally excluded. |

**Extent of Admissibility:**

* **Admissions:** Under **Section 17**, admissions are relevant and can be proved against the person who makes them. They are not "conclusive proof" but act as a "shield" (estoppel).  
* **Confessions:** Admissible only if **voluntary**. Under **Section 23**, confessions to police are inadmissible. However, judicial confessions (before a Magistrate) are highly admissible.

---

**The Genus-Species Relationship**

For your exam, a great way to phrase this is: **"All Confessions are Admissions, but not all Admissions are Confessions."**

* **Admission is the Genus (The broad category):** It can be made in civil or criminal cases and by various interested parties.  
* **Confession is the Species (The specific type):** It is a subset of admission that specifically acknowledges guilt in a criminal matter and is made only by the accused.

**Practical Life Example for Visualisation**

**Scenario A (Admission):** In a civil suit over a boundary wall, your neighbor tells a friend, "Yeah, I think I built the wall two inches into Prateek's land." This is an **Admission** by a party. **Scenario B (Confession):** In a criminal trial for theft, the accused tells the Magistrate, "I climbed the wall and took the gold chain." This is a **Confession** by the accused.

## Question 10(a): Distinguish between Direct and Circumstantial evidences. Can a person be convicted on circumstantial evidence alone?

**Direct Evidence** (**प्रत्यक्ष साक्ष्य** — *Pratyaksh Sakshya*) is evidence that proves a fact in issue directly, without any reasoning or inference.

* **Example:** An eyewitness seeing 'A' stab 'B' is direct evidence of the murder.

**Circumstantial Evidence** (**परिस्थितिजन्य साक्ष्य** — *Paristithijanya Sakshya*) is evidence of various facts which, when connected together, point toward a single conclusion. It is indirect evidence.

* **Example:** No one saw 'A' kill 'B', but 'A' was seen buying a knife, 'A' was seen entering 'B's' house, and 'A' was found with 'B's' blood on his shirt.

**Conviction on Circumstantial Evidence:**

Yes, a person **can be convicted** on circumstantial evidence alone, but the Court follows the **"Five Golden Principles"** (Panchsheel) established in the case of **Sharad Birdhichand Sarda v. State of Maharashtra**:

1. The circumstances must be fully established.  
2. The facts must point **only** toward the guilt of the accused.  
3. The chain of evidence must be so complete that there is no "missing link."  
4. It must exclude every possible hypothesis of innocence.  
5. There must be a "moral certainty" of guilt.

---

## Question 10(b): 'Hearsay evidence is no evidence.' Explain and state the exceptions.

**Hearsay Evidence** (**अनुश्रुत साक्ष्य** — *Anushrut Sakshya*) is "second-hand" evidence. It is a statement made by a witness who did not perceive the fact through their own senses but heard it from someone else. Under **Section 55 BSA**, oral evidence must always be **direct**.

**Why is it excluded?** Because the original speaker cannot be cross-examined, and the person testifying cannot be held responsible for the truth of the statement.

**Exceptions (When Hearsay IS Evidence):**

1. **Res Gestae (Section 4):** Spontaneous statements made during the crime.  
2. **Dying Declaration (Section 26):** The last words of a dying person.  
3. **Admissions and Confessions:** Statements made by the parties themselves.  
4. **Statements in Public Documents:** Records like birth registers or gazettes.  
5. **Expert Opinions (Section 39):** Opinions expressed in books by experts who are dead or unreachable.

## Proved, Disproved, and Not Proved (Section 2(2) BSA)

These terms define the "mental state" of the Judge after seeing the evidence.

**Proved** (**साबित** — *Saabit*): A fact is said to be proved when, after considering the matters before it, the Court either believes it to exist, or considers its existence so probable that a **prudent man** (**विवेकशील व्यक्ति** — *Viveksheel Vyakti*) ought, under the circumstances of the particular case, to act upon the supposition that it exists.

**Disproved** (**नासाबित** — *Naasaabit*): A fact is said to be disproved when the Court, after considering the evidence, either believes it does not exist, or considers its non-existence so probable that a prudent man would act on the belief that it doesn't exist.

**Not Proved** (**साबित नहीं हुआ** — *Saabit Nahi Hua*): This is the middle ground. A fact is "not proved" when it is neither proved nor disproved. It essentially means the evidence was insufficient to tilt the scales of justice in either direction.

---

## Distinction between Res Judicata and Estoppel

Both prevent a person from raising a point again, but they operate differently:

**Res Judicata** (**प्राङ्न्याय** — *Prangnyaya*):

* **Source:** Found in the **Code of Civil Procedure (CPC)**.  
* **Object:** It is based on public policy to end litigation (**Finality**). It stops the **Court** from hearing the same case between the same parties again.  
* **Nature:** It bars the "suit" or the "issue" itself.

**Principle:** Once a matter has been finally decided by a competent court, the same parties cannot litigate the same issue again.

**Example:**  

*Ravi* sues *Sohan* claiming ownership of a piece of land. The court examines evidence and rules that the land belongs to Sohan. Later, Ravi files another suit against Sohan for the same land, raising the same arguments.

👉 The second suit will be barred by **Res Judicata**, because the issue of ownership has already been decided.

**Estoppel** (**विबंध** — *Vibandh*):

* **Source:** Found in the **Law of Evidence (BSA)**.  
* **Object:** It is based on **Equity** and fairness between parties. It stops a **Party** from denying a statement they previously made.  
* **Nature:** It is a "rule of evidence" that shuts the mouth of a party during a trial.

**Principle:** A party cannot deny or contradict a statement they previously made if another party relied on it. It is a rule of evidence.

**Example:**  

*Meena* tells *Arjun* that she will sell him her car for ₹2,00,000. Arjun, relying on her statement, arranges the money. Later, in court, Meena cannot deny that she made the promise if Arjun sues for enforcement.

👉 Meena is **estopped** from denying her earlier representation.

## Presumption as to Dowry Death (Section 118 BSA / Old 113-B IEA)

This is a **Mandatory Presumption** (**विधि की उपधारणा** — *Vidhi ki Upadharna*) designed to tackle social evils.

**The Rule:** When the question is whether a person has committed the dowry death of a woman, and it is shown that **soon before her death** such woman had been subjected by such person to cruelty or harassment for, or in connection with, any demand for dowry, the Court **shall presume** (**उपधारणा करेगा** — *Upadharna Karega*) that such person had caused the dowry death.

**The Significance:** Unlike other presumptions where the Judge has a choice, here the Judge **must** assume the husband/relative is guilty once the "cruelty for dowry" is proved. The burden of proof then shifts to the accused to prove their innocence. This is a **Rebuttable Presumption** (**खंडनीय उपधारणा** — *Khandaniya Upadharna*), meaning the accused can still prove they didn't do it, but the starting point is against them.

## Kinds of Evidence (Section 2 BSA)

For your short note on **Kinds of Evidence** (**साक्ष्य के प्रकार** — *Sakshya ke Prakar*), remember these categories:

1. **Oral Evidence** (**मौखिक साक्ष्य** — *Maukhik Sakshya*): Statements made by witnesses in court.  
2. **Documentary Evidence** (**दस्तावेजी साक्ष्य** — *Dastaveji Sakshya*): Documents or electronic records produced for inspection.  
3. **Primary Evidence** (**प्राथमिक साक्ष्य** — *Prathmik Sakshya*): The original document itself.  
4. **Secondary Evidence** (**द्वितीयक साक्ष्य** — *Dwitiyak Sakshya*): Copies or oral accounts of a document.  
5. **Direct Evidence** (**प्रत्यक्ष साक्ष्य** — *Pratyaksh Sakshya*): Evidence of a fact perceived by the witness's own senses (I saw, I heard).  
6. **Circumstantial Evidence** (**परिस्थितिजन्य साक्ष्य** — *Paristithijanya Sakshya*): Indirect evidence that connects multiple facts to reach a conclusion.  
7. **Hearsay Evidence** (**अनुश्रुत साक्ष्य** — *Anushrut Sakshya*): Second-hand evidence (someone told me), which is generally not admissible.

---

## Probate and Admiralty Jurisdiction (Section 35 BSA / Old 41 IEA)

This deals with **Judgments in Rem** (**सर्वबंधी निर्णय** — *Sarvabandhi Nirnay*). Most judgments only affect the parties in the case (**In Personam**). However, judgments in **Probate** (validity of a Will), **Matrimonial** (divorce/marriage), **Admiralty** (maritime law), or **Insolvency** are "against the whole world."

**Example:** If a Court in its **Probate Jurisdiction** declares a Will to be genuine, that "Fact" is final and binding on everyone in India, not just the family members in court. It is considered **conclusive proof** (**निश्चायक सबूत** — *Nishchayak Saboot*) of the legal character or title mentioned in the judgment.

## Sri Krishna Vs. Kurukshetra University, A.I.R. 1976 S.C.

**Hindi Word:** **साम्यापूर्ण विबंध** (*Saamyapurn Vibandh* — Equitable Estoppel)

This is a landmark case on the principle of **Estoppel**. In this case, a student (Sri Krishna) had a shortage of attendance, but the university allowed him to fill out the examination form and issued him an admit card. After he had taken the exams, the university tried to cancel his results citing the attendance shortage.

The Supreme Court held that the university was **estopped** from doing so. The Court ruled that it was the duty of the university to check the eligibility *before* issuing the admit card. Once the student acted on that admit card and sat for the exam, the university could not "turn back" and change its position. 

## Shall Presume vs. Conclusive Proof (Section 2(2) BSA)

The law of evidence categorizes "presumptions" into different levels of strength:

* **Shall Presume** (**उपधारणा करेगा** — *Upadharna Karega*): This is a **mandatory but rebuttable** presumption. The Court *must* assume the fact is true unless and until the other party provides evidence to "disprove" it.  
  * **Example:** In a **Dowry Death** case (Section 118 BSA), the Court *shall presume* the husband is guilty once the basic facts of cruelty are proven.  
* **Conclusive Proof** (**निश्चायक सबूत** — *Nishchayak Saboot*): This is the strongest form of evidence. When one fact is declared by the BSA to be conclusive proof of another, the Court **shall not allow** any evidence to be given for the purpose of disproving it.  
  * **Example:** A final judgment in a **Probate** or **Matrimonial** case (Section 35 BSA) is conclusive proof of the legal character of the person. You cannot bring new evidence to say a person isn't divorced if a competent court has already issued a decree.

## Judicial Notice (Sections 51 & 52 BSA)

**Hindi Word:** **न्यायिक अनपेक्षित तथ्य** (*Nyayik Anapekshita Tathya*)

Normally, every fact in a case must be proved by evidence. However, **Judicial Notice** is an exception. It refers to facts that are so well-known or obvious that the Court does not require any proof for them.

Under **Section 52**, the Court **shall** take judicial notice of:

* All laws in force in India.  
* The existence and title of states recognized by the Government of India.  
* The seals of all Courts in India.  
* The geographical divisions of the world (e.g., that **Raebareli** is in Uttar Pradesh).  
  **Tip:** In your LG AC case, the Judge takes judicial notice of the Consumer Protection Act; you don't have to "prove" that the law exists.

## Ambiguous Documents (Sections 97–101 BSA)

**Hindi Word:** **संदिग्ध दस्तावेज** (*Sandigdh Dastaavez*)

Sometimes a written document is unclear, and the law has specific rules about whether you can bring outside evidence to explain it:

* **Patent Ambiguity (Section 97):** This is a defect that is obvious on the face of the document. For example, if a contract says, "I will sell my hall for 50,000 or 60,000 rupees." This is so vague that the law says **no evidence** can be given to explain which price was meant. The document is void for uncertainty.  
* **Latent Ambiguity (Sections 98–101):** This is a hidden defect. The document looks fine, but it's hard to apply to real life. For example, "I sell my house in Raebareli to B," but it turns out I own **two** houses in Raebareli. Here, the law **allows** oral evidence to show which house was intended.

## Foreign Law (Section 39 BSA)

**Hindi Word:** **विदेशी विधि** (*Videshi Vidhi*)

Indian Courts are not presumed to know the laws of other countries. Therefore, if a case involves a point of **Foreign Law**, it must be proved as a **Fact**.

* How is it proved? Through the opinion of **Experts** (Section 39\) or by producing books/journals printed under the authority of that foreign government.

## Oral Evidence must be Direct (Section 55 BSA)

**Hindi Word:** **प्रत्यक्ष मौखिक साक्ष्य** (*Pratyaksh Maukhik Sakshya*)

This is the "Golden Rule" of oral evidence. If a witness is testifying about:

* Something they **saw**, they must be the person who saw it.  
* Something they **heard**, they must be the person who heard it.  
* Something they **perceived by any other sense** (like smell), they must be the person who perceived it.  
  This rule is designed to exclude **Hearsay Evidence** (**अनुश्रुत साक्ष्य**). The Court wants to hear from the person who actually experienced the event, not someone who heard a story about it.
